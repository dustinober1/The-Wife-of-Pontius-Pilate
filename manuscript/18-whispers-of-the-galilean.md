# Chapter 18: Whispers of the Galilean

## A Tetrarch's Nerves

The second winter after Sejanus's fall had softened into a spring that smelled, in the praetorium's gardens, of the same salt and citrus it always smelled of, and Claudia had begun to measure the change in her husband less by any single audience than by how rarely he now spoke of Rome at all in the evenings, as though the subject itself had become a wound he preferred not to press. It was Marcus, not Philotas, who brought the first word that reached her of the man from Nazareth, and he brought it apologetically, the way he brought anything he suspected might not be his place to carry.

"A dispatch came from Tiberias, Domina. Not addressed to you, but the Prefect asked that I read it to him twice, which he does not often ask of the routine ones." Marcus held the tablet with the careful two-handed grip he used for anything he judged might matter later. "Herod Antipas writes that a teacher has been going through Galilee, gathering crowds larger than any since the one at the river, and that he has asked his own household more than once whether the man might be John, raised back into the world to trouble him a second time."

Claudia set down the length of wool she had been measuring against the loom's warp. "Raised back into the world."

"Those were his words, Domina, or near enough to them that the scribe who copied the dispatch thought them worth preserving exactly. The Prefect read the line twice himself before he asked me to." Marcus's jaw did the small tightening thing it did when he judged a report to be carrying more weight than its sentences let on, and Claudia, watching it happen, understood that he had already decided what she would decide a moment later: that a tetrarch's superstition about a dead prisoner was itself the least interesting part of the dispatch. The interesting part was what it implied about the size of whatever this teacher had already become, that a man who governed a neighboring tetrarchy on his own authority would trouble himself to write of it at all.

"And what does Pontius make of it."

"He said only that a man Herod fears is not necessarily a man Rome need fear, and that the two questions want answering separately." Marcus hesitated, then added, in the flatter register he used when he had decided a thing was worth saying even though no one had asked him to say it: "He did not sound entirely convinced of his own distinction, Domina, if I may say so."

She let the wool alone for the rest of the afternoon, turning the dispatch's single strange sentence over in her mind the way she had once turned over Philotas's report of a man drawing crowds at the Jordan. This unease sat differently in her. It came from the plain, unmanaged fright of a man who believed, in some part of himself he could not argue out of believing it, that a debt he thought closed had come back around to be collected, and that fright frightened her more than the man himself did.

She found Pontius that evening in the small room off the tablinum where he kept his correspondence, the dispatch already returned to its case but the ledger beside it open to a page he was not reading. "Marcus told me what Herod wrote."

"Then Marcus told you more than the dispatch is worth." Pontius did not look up. "A tetrarch who murdered a prisoner to please a woman's grudge, and has slept badly since, is not a source I intend to build a policy on. Whatever this teacher is, he is Herod's concern before he is mine. Galilee answers to Antipas. Judaea answers to me."

"You read the line twice."

"I read it twice because a man who governs badly out of fear is more dangerous to my province than any preacher could be on his own account. If Herod starts seeing John's ghost in every crowd that gathers within his borders, he will do something rash to quiet his own conscience, and Rome will not care whose jurisdiction the rashness began in when the complaints arrive." He set the stylus down at last and looked at her, and she saw in his face the same weighing calculation she had watched him bring to every crisis of the past year. "I would rather know who this man is before Herod's nerves force my hand in a matter I have not yet had cause to touch."

"And if the man himself is nothing you need fear. Does Herod's fear alone become your problem regardless."

"It becomes my problem the moment his fear makes him do something rash enough that Rome has to notice it, and asks afterward why the neighboring prefect said nothing while it was still small enough to watch quietly." He returned to the ledger before him, though she noticed he did not resume writing in it. "I would rather spend an evening reading a dispatch twice than spend a season cleaning up after a tetrarch's guilty conscience."

## What Tamar Had Already Heard

Tamar, when Claudia raised it with her that evening, had the same unhurried manner she always brought to news the household considered urgent and she did not, though this time Claudia thought she saw something new beneath the unhurriedness, a carefulness that had not been there when she spoke of John.

"They call him Jesus, of Nazareth, though he teaches now mostly around the lake at Capernaum," Tamar said, working a knot from a length of thread with the patience she gave everything small. "My cousin's wife has kin near there. She says he was heard once in their own synagogue, reading the scroll and then sitting down to explain it as though the words applied to the room he was standing in and not only to the old prophets who wrote them, which she says is not how the teachers usually do it."

"Is that what draws the crowds. The manner of his teaching."

"Partly. But the manner would not draw thousands on its own, Domina. It is the other reports that draw them, the ones I do not know how to weigh for you honestly, because I was not there to see them and neither was anyone I trust enough to repeat it as fact rather than hope." Tamar looked up from the thread. "My cousin's wife says he heals the complaints a family has already stopped expecting to see mended, the ones a physician has given up on and gone home from. And she says he eats at the tables of the tax farmers, the ones who collect for Rome and are hated for it more than Rome itself is hated. He sits with them openly, she says, as though what it costs him among the rest were a small price already paid and settled."

"Does she say why. A teacher gains nothing among his own people by sitting at that table."

"She does not know why, Domina, only that he does it, and that it is the detail her husband's kin repeat most often when they speak of him, more than the healings even, because a healing can be doubted or explained away by a hundred means, but a respected teacher choosing to sit where he is not respected for it is a thing a whole village can watch happen with its own eyes and argue about for a month afterward."

Claudia thought of John, waist-deep in the Jordan, telling tax collectors only to take no more than what was owed and sending them home to go on collecting it. "That is not John's way. John told them to repent of the thing and continue in it honestly. This man sits down beside them as though the thing were already forgiven before they asked."

"Your cousin Yaakov would have an opinion on that, I think, if anyone in your family would." Claudia had not forgotten the shawl three rows back from the standards, nor the shoulder he had carried bruised through the aqueduct riot afterward.

"He does, Domina, though it is not the opinion you would expect of a man who has twice stood where a Roman club could have found him." Tamar allowed herself something close to a smile. "Yaakov says a man who forgives a tax farmer before the tax farmer has repented is either a fool or the only honest man left in Galilee, because every other teacher he has ever heard demands the repentance first and offers the forgiveness only afterward, as a wage owed for good behavior, and Yaakov says he has grown tired, after two beatings for a cause he still believes was just, of waiting for wages that never quite arrive on the day they are promised."

"That sounds less like theology than exhaustion."

"It is both, Domina. I have found, since Rivkah's mourning, that they are very often the same thing wearing two different faces, and that a man exhausted enough stops caring which face is doing the talking, so long as something finally answers him."

"That is what unsettles the Pharisees who write to my cousin's kin about it, yes. John asked the world to clean itself before the flood came. This one behaves, my cousin's wife says, as though the flood has already receded and the only task left is to notice it." Tamar set the thread down entirely now, and Claudia understood she had reached the part of the report she trusted least and wanted least to simplify. "I do not know if it is true, Domina. I know only that the same hope I gave you at Rivkah's mourning, the hope that what is broken is not broken forever, is the hope people say they feel standing in a crowd around this man, and I have never before heard that hope described as something a person could stand near and feel, not only carry alone in a house after a burial."

## Simon's Silence

It was Simon who gave her the report she trusted most, though he gave it to her reluctantly, three days later, when she found him idle by the kitchen well longer than his duties required and asked him directly what kept him there.

"My uncle wrote, Domina." Simon had grown in the years since the Jordan, though he still carried the same quiet stillness she remembered from the evening he had come home baptized and would not speak of it for a week. "He says some who followed John to the river have gone now to hear this other teacher instead. He says it as though it troubles him, that men who wept at John's death should so soon go looking for someone to stand in the space he left."

"Does it trouble you."

He did not answer at once, turning the bucket rope in his hands the way he had once turned his own guilt over surviving a mentor he loved. "John told us the world would end unless we mended it first. This teacher, they say, tells people the mending has already begun, and asks them only to notice it and live as though it were true." Simon looked up at her, and Claudia saw in his face the same unresolved grief she had first seen there the week John died, still working itself out toward some shape it had not yet found. "I do not know if that is a comfort or a wound, Domina. John gave his whole life to say the world must change before it can be forgiven. If this man is right that it need not wait, then John spent everything he had on a fear that was already answered before he ever waded into the river to preach it."

She had no comfort ready for him, and did not offer a false one. "Perhaps a man can be right about the hunger and still not live to see the feast laid," she said instead, and Simon nodded slowly, as though the thought cost him something to accept.

"You have grown wiser than the boy who came back from the Jordan with a sunburn and nothing to say for a week," Claudia said, watching him turn the bucket rope over once more.

"I do not feel wiser, Domina. I feel as though I have simply been given a second grief before I finished with the first, and no one has yet told me whether the two are meant to comfort each other or only to sit side by side, unreconciled, for as long as I carry them." He managed something close to a smile, brief and unpracticed. "My uncle says a man does not need every grief resolved before he goes on living through the next one. I have found that easier to hear than to believe, these past days."

"My uncle says one more thing, Domina, though I do not know what weight to give it." He set the bucket down at last. "He says some who knew John at the river swear this teacher was among those John himself baptized, before either of them was known beyond his own village. If that is true, then whatever John began at the water, this man was standing in the current for it too, long before either of them had a name worth reporting to a garrison."

She walked back through the colonnade turning it over, the image of two unknown men standing together in the same brown water years before either had drawn a crowd large enough to trouble a prefect's steward. She could not decide whether the two stories joining at so plain a source comforted her, or only deepened the sense that something had been building toward this season for far longer than the household's recent rumors accounted for.

## The Name Claudia Kept

By that night she had begun repeating the name to herself without quite deciding to: Jesus of Nazareth, the Galilean, the teacher who sat at forbidden tables and told crowds that the world's mending had already started somewhere they could not yet fully see. She had heard rumors of prophets before, in six years of living among a people who seemed, to her Roman eye, to expect the world's correction the way other peoples expected only its continuation. None of the earlier rumors had lodged in her the way this one had, quietly, without her permission, in the same part of her mind she kept for things she had not yet found the shape to speak of aloud.

She did not raise Simon's report with Pontius that evening, though she heard him, before he slept, mutter half a sentence about a tetrarch's nerves being no basis for a provincial policy. Herod's dispatch had lodged in him too, she thought, in whatever part of a magistrate's mind kept watch for the next crisis before it had fully announced itself. Marcus, passing through the room to draw the lamps down for the night, paused at the doorway a moment longer than the errand required, and Claudia, watching him go, read in the pause the wariness of a man standing near a door he suspected was about to open, whether or not anyone in the room had yet decided to walk through it.

She lay awake longer than she meant to, turning the name over once more before sleep took her, Jesus of Nazareth, the Galilean. She could not have said, if asked, why a stranger's name from a province she had never visited should feel, in the dark, less like news arriving from outside her life than like something she had been waiting, without knowing it, to hear named at last.
