# Chapter 28: Gerizim

## The Gathering at the Mountain

The report that reached him in the early spring carried a number large enough that Pontius did not trouble, this time, to have Philotas draw him a rough sketch of the ground before he acted on it, a departure from his own long habit that he did not, in the moment, think to question.

"Several thousand, Prefect, and armed, gathering at the village of Tirathana at the foot of the mountain, meaning to climb to the summit together and dig there for the sacred vessels their prophet swears Moses buried on the mountain before he died." Rufus delivered the number with the same flat competence he had always brought to a report, though Pontius heard in it, or believed he heard in it, an urgency Rufus had not troubled to sharpen in years. "The Samaritans hold the mountain sacred above any temple, Prefect, and a prophet promising to uncover something that sacred, at the head of an armed multitude that size, is not a matter I would counsel waiting to measure further."

Pontius weighed the report for the length of a single breath, a fraction of the time he would once have given a crowd's true danger before he answered it, and found the shortness of his own weighing did not trouble him the way it once would have. Three years of closing doors quickly had taught him a different arithmetic than the one Philotas's careful sketches had once trained him to trust, an arithmetic that asked only how large the danger might become if left to gather further, never how large it presently was, and by that faster, harder measure the report before him required no further study at all. "Armed men climbing a mountain together, at the word of a prophet who promises them something the priesthood in Jerusalem would call blasphemy if it were found. That is not a pilgrimage, Rufus. That is the beginning of an uprising dressed in a scripture's clothing, and I do not intend to let it finish dressing before I answer it."

## The Decision

He gave the order within the hour, cavalry and a body of infantry both, to move against the gathering at Tirathana before the climb to the summit could properly begin, a decision he made standing in the same outer court where he had once, years before, weighed a report of five thousand fed peaceably beside a different water and found, in that weighing, no cause for a Roman hand to act at all.

He did not, this time, ask Philotas to sketch him the ground, or ask Rufus whether the gathered men had spoken against Rome, against tribute, against anything a governor was properly obliged to answer for. He did not ask, this time, whether the crowd's own conduct so far gave any sign of what it intended once it reached whatever it had gathered to find. He asked only how many men had assembled, and where, and how quickly a Roman force could reach them before the numbers grew past the point a single decisive action could still contain, and found, answering his own question, that the answer satisfied him completely, the way a ledger's clean closing had once satisfied him before he had learned, at a different tribunal three years past, how little a clean closing actually proved.

*A governor who waits to be certain waits until certainty no longer helps him*, he told himself, mounting the horse Rufus had already ordered saddled, and did not trouble, in the saddle, to ask himself when precisely he had traded the theory of an even hand, evenly applied, for a theory that asked only how fast a threat could be ended before it had the chance to prove itself a threat at all.

He thought, riding out at the head of the column, of Claudia's voice the week before, asking him whether he was certain a fool's errand and a rebellion were as easily told apart as his own dismissal of the matter had made them sound, and found the memory of the question arrived in him now as a small, distant irritation rather than as anything he judged worth the delay of turning back to weigh more carefully. She had not seen the report. She had not seen the numbers Rufus placed before him that morning, numbers that made the mountain's gathering read, to a man who had learned across three hard years to trust speed over patience, as considerably more dangerous than any crowd she had ever watched him measure from a terrace. He put the question from his mind before the column had cleared the city gate, and did not think of it again until it was already too late for the thinking to change anything the day still meant to make of him.

## What Happened at the Mountain

The cavalry reached the base of the ascent before the gathered Samaritans had climbed more than a third of the way toward the summit, and what followed, by every account that reached him afterward, bore no resemblance to the battle his own hurried arithmetic had prepared him to expect: a slaughter of men dressed for a pilgrimage, caught by soldiers braced for a war.

"They were not armed for a fight, Prefect, whatever weapons some among them carried for the road's own ordinary dangers." Rufus gave him the fuller account that evening, in the same flat register he had used to deliver every hard report of the past ten years, though Pontius thought he heard, beneath the flatness this time, something closer to a soldier's own private unease at what his orders had produced. "Many scattered at the first charge and were cut down running rather than fighting. The leaders who did not flee, we took alive and have since executed, on your standing order that any man found inciting an armed gathering against Roman order forfeits his life without further hearing."

"How many dead."

"More than the fighting alone accounts for, Prefect. Some in the panic of the retreat, trampled or fallen from the mountain's own steep ground rather than struck by any Roman blade. I could not give you an exact count tonight that I would trust by morning." Rufus held his gaze a moment longer than the report required. "I do not think, Prefect, whatever the numbers finally settle to, that this will read to the Samaritan council, or to Vitellius above them, as a rebellion justly answered. I think it will read as several thousand unarmed pilgrims answered by cavalry before anyone troubled to learn what they had actually gathered to do."

Pontius did not answer that at once, and found, turning Rufus's account over, that he could locate in himself none of the searching, close attention he had once given every crisis of his early years in this office, only a flat and exhausted certainty that the order had been correct when he gave it and remained correct now, regardless of what its cost turned out, on a fuller accounting, to have actually been. He told himself this several times that evening, alone, the way he had once told himself, drafting the dispatch after the tribunal, that a ledger's clean closing was proof enough of a thing done rightly. He found the telling came less easily to him this time than it ever had before, though he could not, that first night, have said precisely why a mountain full of Samaritan dead should sit differently in him than a single Galilean's cross had sat three years before, only that it did, and that the difference itself frightened him more than either death alone had managed to.

## The Council's Complaint

The Samaritan council's delegation reached Vitellius in Antioch within the month, faster than any Judaean grievance had ever traveled in Pontius's ten years of governing beneath the legate's distant authority, and the substance of their complaint, when it finally reached Pontius secondhand, through a dispatch considerably less flattering than any he had ever drafted about himself, named the mountain gathering exactly as Rufus had warned him it would be named: an unarmed pilgrimage, seeking nothing but a buried relic sacred to their own faith, answered by a governor's cavalry before any crime had been committed beyond the crime of climbing their own mountain in numbers a nervous Roman report had mistaken for an army.

"Vitellius has ordered you to Rome, Prefect, to answer to the Emperor directly for what happened at the mountain, and has sent Marcellus ahead to hold the province in the meanwhile." Rufus delivered this final report with none of the flatness he had managed for every report before it, and Pontius understood, hearing the strain in his oldest officer's voice, that Rufus had served him faithfully through every crisis of ten hard years and had never once, before this morning, had to deliver him a summons that ended the serving.

Pontius took the dispatch and read it through twice, the same close reading he had once given every hard report before three years of closing doors quickly had trained him out of the habit, and found himself now, reading Vitellius's plain and unadorned language a second time, doing the very thing that habit had cost him doing at the mountain itself, weighing every word for what it actually said rather than for how quickly he could file it away and move on to the next demand on his attention. It said, when he had finished weighing it fully, that a legate of Syria who answered to Rome directly had judged a governor of Judaea to have failed at the single thing his whole theory of rule had once claimed to guarantee an even hand, applied evenly, that measured every threat by its true size rather than by a frightened man's hurried arithmetic for how fast the threat could be made to disappear.

## What a Map Could No Longer Show Him

He sat alone with the dispatch a long while after Rufus withdrew, and found himself thinking, for the first time since that tribunal morning, of the compass Claudia had pressed into his hand in a garden in Rome, and of her old accusation that he had crossed the sea to govern a people whose pride he had never troubled to map.

He understood now, turning the accusation over with a clarity that arrived far too late to be of any use to the men who had died on the mountain's steep ground, that he had spent his first six years in this office failing to map a conquered people's pride, and had spent the three years since failing at something worse, refusing to map his own fear once it had grown large enough to answer every threat by the same fast, hard measure regardless of what the measure actually required of the men standing beneath it. A governor who feared nothing measured wrongly by ignorance. A governor who feared everything measured wrongly by haste. He had believed, crossing from one failure into the other three years past, that he had learned something true about how a province like this one actually worked. He understood now, with Vitellius's summons open on the desk before him and a mountain full of dead men who had wanted nothing more than a buried relic, that he had only traded one blindness for another, and that the province, the office, and the wife who had once told him plainly what he had failed to map would all outlast whatever accounting Rome now meant to extract from him for the trading.
