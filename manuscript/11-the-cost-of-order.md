# Chapter 11: The Cost of Order

## The Silence After

The household returned to Caesarea within the week, sooner than the festival calendar strictly required, and Claudia understood the early departure for what it was without either of them saying so: a city with blood still being scrubbed from its colonnade stones was not a city Pontius wished his household to go on living in while the scrubbing continued. He did not speak of the riot again after that first night in her doorway, and she did not raise it, and the silence between them settled over the sea crossing back down the coast road with the particular density of a thing both of them were carrying and neither would set down first, for fear of what the other might do with it once it was on the table between them.

She watched him instead in the small ways a wife watches a husband she has decided to keep observing, not confronting: the way he took his meals at his desk more often than at the table now, the dispatches piled at his elbow in a stack that never seemed to shrink no matter how many hours he gave it, the new habit of standing at the study window with his back to the room for long stretches that had nothing to do with anything he could be reading there. He had always worked long hours. This was different, and she recognized the difference the way she recognized weather changing before the sky itself confirmed it: a man occupying his hours rather than spending them, filling the space where a reckoning might otherwise have room to happen.

Tamar came to her twice in that first week, once with news of Yaakov, mending faster than the physician had promised though the ribs still caught his breath on cold mornings, and once with nothing to report at all, simply to sit with Claudia in the peristyle garden in a silence that had none of the strain the household's other silence carried. Claudia understood, sitting with her, that whatever had passed between them in the doorway the night of the riot, the word Tamar had used, subject, had not been withdrawn and was not going to be, only folded into the friendship as a fact the friendship would now have to hold, impossible to pretend away. She found she preferred it that way. A friendship that pretended away the actual shape of the world between them would have been, she thought, no friendship worth the name.

## A Show of Unity

A delegation of Caesarea's Greek magistrates had been promised a formal dinner before the riot, a courtesy planned months in advance to smooth relations with the city's non-Jewish council, and Pontius would not cancel it, arguing over supper the night before that a governor who let one bad season in Jerusalem unsettle every other obligation on his calendar taught every faction in the province that unsettling him was worth the attempt. Claudia did not disagree with the reasoning. She disagreed, privately, with what the evening would ask of her, and found she had no way to say so that did not sound, even to her own ear, like a wife shirking a duty she had performed without complaint a dozen times before.

She wore the blue stola she kept for occasions requiring the most deliberate kind of composure, and stood beside her husband at the door of the triclinium greeting men who spoke of the aqueduct works with the easy approval of people who had not stood in the colonnade's shade to watch what the works had cost, praising the Prefect's foresight in securing Temple funds for a project that would serve Greek and Jew alike, and Claudia smiled at each of them in turn and said the small correct things a prefect's wife said on such evenings, and felt, doing it, a distance opening in herself that had nothing to do with the guests and everything to do with the ease of her own performance. She had not known, until that evening, how well she had been trained to this. She had been doing it since the tablinum, since before she had a name for what she was doing: making herself useful by making a room comfortable, whatever the room's comfort cost her elsewhere.

Pontius performed his own part of the evening with the same practiced competence, laughing at the right moments, deflecting a magistrate's pointed question about the crowd's numbers with a figure that was technically accurate and told the man nothing he had actually asked, and only once, catching Claudia's eye across the room while a servant refilled his cup, did something pass between them that was not performance: a flicker of the same exhaustion she was carrying herself, acknowledged and immediately folded back beneath the evening's required smoothness, gone before either of them could decide what to do with having seen it in the other. It was, she thought later, the most honest exchange they had all evening, and it had taken the length of a heartbeat and no words at all.

The magistrates left well satisfied, by every measure the evening had been built to satisfy them by, and Claudia and Pontius stood afterward in the emptied triclinium among the debris of a dinner neither of them had wanted, not speaking, until he said, finally, in a voice stripped of the whole evening's polish, "I am tired of performing certainty I no longer entirely feel."

"Then stop performing it. At least with me."

"I do not know how to do one without the other anymore. I have been the man who is certain for so long that I am not sure what is left of me if I set the certainty down even for an evening, even in my own house, even with you." He said it plainly, without the defensiveness she had braced for, and she understood, hearing it, that this was as close as he was likely to come for some time to admitting what the letter from Sejanus would only deepen a week later: that the account he gave the world and the account he gave himself had come apart somewhere in the colonnade's shade, and he did not yet know how, or whether, to bring them back together.

## A Letter from the Palatine

The letter from Sejanus reached Caesarea on a ship out of Ostia three weeks after the riot, later than the first garbled rumors of it had already reached Rome by other channels, and Pontius read it standing in the study with the seal already broken before Claudia entered, his face arranged into the particular stillness she had learned meant he was working to keep some other expression from showing through it.

"He is not displeased," Pontius said, before she had asked, and held the letter out to her with a steadiness that cost him something to maintain. "Read it yourself. I would rather you heard it from him than from my account of it."

*Sejanus to Pontius Pilatus, greeting. Word has reached the Palatine, as such word always does, of unrest at your tribunal over the matter of the aqueduct, and of losses among the provincials in the course of putting it down. I write to say plainly what I would say to any commander of mine who had faced a crowd pressing against lawful authority and had not flinched from the cost of holding it: you did what the office required. A prefect who cannot compel order from a province is a prefect Rome has no further use for, whatever the province itself thinks of the compelling. I have said as much, where it wanted saying, to those in the Senate who would make a season's grievance out of a governor's competence. Continue the works. Continue the water. A city that remembers a channel running clean a decade from now will not trouble itself to remember who among its own crowd chose defiance over the Prefect's plain offer to disperse in peace. See that the treasury accounts are kept beyond any priest's later complaint, and write to me again only if Rome's attention should shift, which for now it has not, and which I do not expect it to.*

Claudia read it twice, the second time more slowly than the first, searching the careful Latin for some acknowledgment of the thing it was actually about, some word that named the old man in the colonnade or the woman screaming a name into the dust or Yaakov's ribs still catching on cold mornings, and found instead only the arithmetic she had already heard once from her husband's own mouth, dressed now in a patron's confident hand and returned to him as absolution he had not, as far as she could tell, asked for but would not refuse either.

"He calls it competence," she said, setting the letter down on the desk between them rather than handing it back, so that neither of them would have to touch it a moment longer than necessary. "He does not call it anything else. Not the dead. Not what it cost the city to learn it could be done to them twice."

"He is a patron writing to the man whose career he has staked his own judgment on. What would you have him write instead?"

"I do not know what I would have him write. I know what I would have you feel, reading it, and I do not think it is relief."

Pontius was quiet long enough that she thought he might not answer at all, and when he did, his voice had gone to the low, careful register she had heard only once before, in her doorway the night of the riot itself. "I felt relief," he said. "For the length of about a sentence, before I understood what I was feeling relief about, and then I felt something considerably worse than relief, and I have not managed, in three weeks, to feel anything since that resolves the two of those into a single honest account of myself. Sejanus has given me the account Rome requires. I do not know yet whether it is the account I require, and I find I am in no hurry to discover that it is not, because I do not know what I would do with myself once I did."

It was the most he had said to her since the night of the riot, and she understood, hearing it, that it was also the closest he would come to admitting what she had already seen clearly enough from the study doorway three weeks running: that the letter had not settled anything in him, only given him a formal document he could set beside the unsettled thing and call the matter, officially, closed.

## What She Kept

She began keeping the household's accounts herself that autumn, a task she had left mostly to Marcus and the steward's ledgers in the years before, and told Pontius only that she wanted the practice of it, a reason true enough on its face that he did not think to question it further. The truer reason she kept from him, the way she was keeping most things from him now without quite naming the keeping as a decision: with her own hand on the ledger she could see exactly where every sum in the house went, could set aside what she needed to set aside without a steward's questions, and could feel, doing the sums herself late at night by lamplight, something closer to the useful stillness the tablinum lessons had once promised her and never quite delivered, now that the use she was putting it to was entirely her own.

She wrote her own letter that same week, though she showed it to no one and sent it nowhere, a habit she had not practiced since the tablinum lessons of her childhood, when her guardian's Greek tutor had set her copying model letters to imaginary correspondents as an exercise in a clear hand, the content never anything she actually meant to say. This one she meant entirely, and burned in the study brazier before the ink had fully dried, because she could not think of a single person alive to whom she could send an honest account of what she had watched happen in the colonnade's shade without either endangering the person who received it or asking of them a grief they had no obligation to carry on her behalf.

*I stood forty yards from the men who did it*, the letter had said, before she fed it to the flame, *and told myself the distance was a kind of caution. I have come to understand it was a kind of cowardice wearing caution's clothes, and I do not know how a woman is meant to go on keeping a household and a marriage and the ordinary courtesies of a Roman wife once she has understood a thing like that about herself and cannot unlearn it. And the water was mine before it was his. I asked for a mercy without asking what it would cost the people receiving it, or whose money it truly was, and I do not know which is the harder thing to carry: that I stood too far away to stop it, or that I was close enough, months before, to have started it.*

She did not tell Pontius she had written it, and did not tell him, either, about the small private practice she began that same week and kept, in one form or another, for the rest of her time in Judaea: a coin set aside from the household accounts, not much, the kind of sum that would not be missed or questioned, sent through Tamar to whatever family in the city could be found still burying its dead from the riot without the means to do it properly. It was not restitution. She understood that clearly enough not to flatter herself with the word. It was closer to the ink-stained crescent she still checked beneath her thumbnail some mornings from a lesson learned in a Roman tablinum before she had ever seen this province: a way of proving to herself, in some small ledger no one else would ever audit, that she had been of actual use to someone, not merely present at the accounting of them.

Marcus knew. He carried the coin himself twice, when Tamar's own errands could not cover it, and said nothing to Pontius about where the household's smaller sums had begun quietly going. Claudia had not asked that discretion of him. Watching his face on the evenings he returned from those errands, she read it as a private accounting of his own, kept the way she kept hers, in a ledger neither of them would ever show the other in full.

By autumn the marriage had settled into a new shape neither of them named aloud, cordial where it had once been close, a household still run with the same competence and the same outward ease, but crossed now by a silence that had nowhere left to go once it had already been spoken once, in a doorway, and answered once, in a letter neither of them could unread. Claudia found she did not entirely mourn the change. A marriage that had survived on a shared certainty about the arithmetic of governing could not, she thought, have survived intact the discovery that the arithmetic left men dead in a colonnade and called the sum, afterward, competence. What remained instead was a harder thing, less comfortable and more honest, two people who had each seen something in themselves that summer they had not expected to see, and had chosen, for reasons neither fully understood yet, to go on sharing a household and a bed and an uncertain future in the same province regardless.

She understood, watching him some evenings from across the study, that whatever Pontius had once meant by governing, an even hand and an unbending spine, had begun that season to mean something narrower and more exhausted, a man holding a line because the line was the only shape of himself he still knew how to occupy. She did not yet know what she meant by anything herself, watching him from across a room grown quieter than it had been in Rome. She knew only that she could no longer watch from forty yards and call it caution, and that whatever came next in this province, she intended to be closer to it than that, whatever the closeness cost her.
