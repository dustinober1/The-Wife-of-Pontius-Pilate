# Chapter 20: The Second Dream

## The Cracking Sound Returns

It came back on a night thick with the first real heat of the Passover season, nearly two years since it had last troubled her sleep, and Claudia woke gasping in a way the first nights had never quite managed, her linen tangled around her legs as though her body had fought the dream even while her mind still believed itself only watching it.

The height was the same, still without a fixed shape she could name on waking, terrace or hillside or neither, and the cracking sound came at the same instant it always had, sharp as stone giving way under ice too thin to bear a season's weight. But the edge had sharpened this time in a manner the earlier nights had never allowed her. She could see now that Pontius walked toward it with his shoulders set the way she had watched them set a hundred times in six years of audiences she had witnessed from a gallery or a doorway, a man composing himself for a decision he had not yet admitted, even to himself, that he had already made. And the presence at the edge, which had stood in the earlier dreams as no more than a stillness without a shape, had gathered, this time, the unmistakable outline of a man: bound at the wrists, she thought, though she could not afterward have said how she knew it, upright in a way that read less as defiance than as something closer to patience, waiting the way a man waits who has already decided what the waiting will cost him and finds the cost, somehow, no longer able to touch him.

She did not see a face. She understood, lying rigid in the dark afterward with her heart still going hard against her ribs, that this was the detail she kept returning to even as the rest of the dream dissolved around it, that the figure had turned toward her in the last instant before the sound woke her, exactly as it always had, and that she had felt herself seen by it in a way she had no vocabulary yet to describe, without once being permitted to see, in return, what it was that had seen her.

Pontius slept on beside her, his breathing slow and untroubled, and she lay a long while watching the ceiling rather than him this time, pressing her thumb against the seam of her palm until the small physical fact of doing it steadied her enough to trust her own legs to carry her to the window.

## What Marcus Saw

Marcus brought the watered wine again the following morning without being asked, setting it at her elbow while she sat with the household accounts open before her and made no pretense of reading them, and she noticed, accepting the cup, that he lingered a moment longer than the errand required before he withdrew to the doorway.

"You slept poorly again, Domina." He said it carefully, in the register he used for observations he had decided were worth making even though no one had invited him to make them. "It is the second such morning this season."

"It is the heat, Marcus. Passover always brings it early." She heard, saying it, how thin the excuse had worn between them, the same excuse she had given herself two years before and had believed, then, considerably more than she believed it now.

He did not press the matter, only inclined his head in the small way he had of accepting an answer he had not been convinced by. She carried, watching him withdraw, the same thought she had carried since the first dream: that whatever he read of the household's private weather he read accurately and kept to himself with a discretion she had never once had cause to doubt, and that his silence on the matter was its own kind of witness, the sort that asked nothing of her and offered, in its place, only the quiet fact of having noticed.

The ink-stain was still faint on his own thumb after all these years, a mirror of the one beneath her own thumbnail that had never fully faded since childhood, and she wondered, not for the first time, whether the two marks meant the same thing on two such different hands, or whether a mark of usefulness read differently depending on who was still free to set the usefulness down and who was not.

## The Page She Almost Kept

She sat with a fresh page before her that same evening, in the same private practice she had kept since the letter she burned after the colonnade riot, and found the words came harder this time than they had the first, as though the dream itself had grown too particular to be reduced honestly to the language she had used two years before.

*I do not believe in omens*, she wrote, and stopped, the pen resting against the page a long moment before she pressed on. *I believed that once and found it easier to believe when the dream gave me nothing but a stillness with no shape. It has a shape now. I do not know what to do with a fear that has begun, on its own account and without my permission, to take a shape.*

She read the lines back once, in the lamplight, and found she did not trust herself to finish the thought honestly without writing something she was not yet ready to have exist anywhere outside her own head, even in a private hand meant for no other eyes. She held the page to the flame before the ink had fully set, watching the words blacken and curl past reading, and told herself, as she had told herself the first time, that the burning was only for privacy's sake, though she noted, this time, that the telling cost her more effort to believe than it had two years before.

## A Conversation with Tamar

She found Tamar in the kitchen court sorting the last of the winter's dried figs from the ones already turning, and sat with her longer than the errand that had brought her there required, watching Tamar's hands work through the baskets with the same unhurried attention she brought to everything.

"You have not asked me about the teacher in some months, Domina," Tamar said, without looking up, in the manner she had of naming a thing before Claudia had found the words to raise it herself. "I think you have been turning something over privately. You have that look about you that you had the week before you first asked me whether dreams meant anything."

Claudia let that sit a moment before she answered it, choosing, as she had learned to do across years of these conversations, to answer the honest part of the question, not the part that asked her to explain herself fully. "What is the latest word from your cousin's kin."

"That he is coming south, Domina, with the feast. Not alone. A great crowd travels with him now, by every account, larger with each town he passes through on the road, and my cousin's wife writes that some in the crowd have taken to calling him son of David when they think a Roman ear is not close enough to hear it clearly." Tamar set a fig aside, turning after it to the next. "A blind man near Jericho, she says, called out to him by that name in the open road, loudly enough that the crowd tried to silence him for it, and the teacher stopped anyway and gave him his sight back rather than let the crowd's caution decide who deserved to be heard."

"Son of David." Claudia turned the phrase over, feeling its weight without yet being able to name, precisely, what the weight consisted of. "That is not the same as a teacher who heals a family's private grief in Galilee. That is a claim, or near enough to one that a crowd repeating it in the road might as well be making it for him."

"It is, Domina, and I think that is exactly what troubles the council more than anything he has said himself. A man can refuse to claim a title. He cannot as easily refuse a crowd that has already decided to give it to him, especially not in a city already primed, every Passover, to remember that it was promised a king once and has been waiting since for the promise to come due." Tamar looked up at last, and Claudia saw in her face the same careful reserve she had worn discussing John before his death, the reserve of a woman who had already watched one man's following end at the point of a sword and did not trust herself to hope aloud for a different outcome twice. "I do not know if he is the one, Domina. I have told you before, I do not think that is a question I am equipped to answer from a kitchen court in Caesarea. I know only that the hope people carry toward him now is larger than the hope they carried toward John, because John asked them to prepare for something still arriving, and this one, by every account, behaves as though it has already begun arriving in him."

Claudia thought of the figure at the edge in the dream, bound at the wrists, upright, patient in a way that had unsettled her more than any violence could have. Sitting in the ordinary daylight of the kitchen court with the figs' sweet decay heavy in the air, she could not decide whether the thought that had risen in her, unbidden, connecting the two, was a true seeing or only a frightened woman's mind reaching for a shape to fit an unease that had none of its own. She did not speak the thought aloud. She was not yet ready to hear, in Tamar's careful voice, whatever answer the saying of it might return to her.

"You have gone quiet, Domina," Tamar said, setting the last basket aside. "You do that when a thing has occurred to you that you are not yet willing to test against another person's opinion of it."

"I am not certain it is a thought worth testing yet. I am not certain it is a thought at all, and not only a fear wearing a thought's clothing to make itself easier to carry." Claudia set a fig back in the basket without eating it. "I will tell you when I trust it enough to survive being said aloud. I do not trust it yet."

"Then I will not ask again until you bring it to me yourself." Tamar studied her a moment longer, the same careful reading she had once given Claudia's first oblique question about dreams, years before. "I will only say, Domina, that in my experience a fear a person cannot yet say aloud is usually the truest one they carry, precisely because it has not yet been shaped by anyone else's opinion of it. The ones we can say easily are usually the ones we have already half talked ourselves out of."

## Two Weeks to Jerusalem

She did not tell Pontius about the dream that night either, watching him instead across the supper table as he read through the last of the season's dispatches with the flat attention he gave every piece of correspondence that had not yet resolved itself into either a crisis or a settled matter. The shape at the edge of her dream stayed with her regardless, a thought she could not put down even as she reached for the bread and answered some ordinary question of his about the household's preparations for the journey.

"Two weeks," he said, not looking up from the dispatch, "and we travel up for the feast, the same as every year." He said it in the tone of a man reciting a fixture of the calendar, giving no sign it was a decision that troubled him, though she noted, watching the line of his shoulders across the table, a tension in them that had nothing to do with the dispatch he held. "Rufus tells me the roads are already thick with pilgrims earlier than usual this season. A crowd larger than any Jerusalem has hosted in years, by his count, and a teacher somewhere inside it that half the city already seems inclined to call by a title I would rather no one in this province felt free to use of any man not wearing purple."

"You have written to Herod about him already. Eleazar has warned you the council watches him closely. What more is there to decide, before the feast even begins."

"Whether the watching stays the council's business, or becomes mine." He set the dispatch down at last, and she saw in his face the same weighing calculation she had watched him bring to every crisis since Sejanus fell, sharpened now by a season's practice into something closer to habit than to effort. "A crowd this size, arriving in a city already primed by the feast to remember every promise it has ever been made about a king, is not a matter I can leave entirely to a council that would rather my hand did the raising for them, whatever they tell me to my face about not asking it of me. I would rather arrive in Jerusalem having already decided how I mean to answer that question than be asked it for the first time in the middle of a crowd I can no longer control."

"And how do you mean to answer it."

"By waiting to see what the man himself does with the title, before I decide what I will do about it." He picked the dispatch back up, though she noticed he did not resume reading it. "A crowd can call a man anything it likes on a public road. It is only a danger to me the day the man being called it accepts the naming for himself, or fails to refuse it plainly enough that a garrison nervous for its own reasons mistakes his silence for a claim."

She said nothing of the dream, and nothing of the blind man on the Jericho road, and nothing of the shape at the edge that had turned toward her before the sound woke her, though she felt, sitting across from him in the ordinary lamplight with two weeks still standing between this evening and whatever waited at the end of the road to Jerusalem, that some part of her had already begun walking toward that road on its own account, ahead of her body, ahead of anything she yet had the courage or the vocabulary to call it.
