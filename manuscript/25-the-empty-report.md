# Chapter 25: The Empty Report

## The Petition

The councillor came to him in the last hour before the Sabbath began, alone, without the retinue Caiaphas always brought and without any of the careful procedural language the Temple's business usually arrived wrapped in, and Pontius understood, receiving him in the small audience room rather than the hall, that this was a man who had come to ask something of him privately instead of negotiating on the council's behalf.

"Joseph, of Arimathea." The man gave his name plainly, deferential the way a petitioner is with a magistrate, not the way a councillor is with an equal, and Pontius noted the deference with the same close attention he gave every small signal a room offered him before its business had properly begun. "I sat on the council, Prefect, though I did not consent to what it decided last night. I have come to ask for the body, that it might be taken down and buried before the Sabbath finds it still hanging where the law would otherwise leave it exposed."

"You understand what you are asking. A body claimed by a council member, buried in a tomb of that council member's own choosing, rather than sent to the ground the law provides for men condemned as this one was condemned." Pontius said it as a statement, not a challenge, watching the man's face for whatever calculation lay beneath the request, the same searching attention he had given every petition of his six years in this office, and found, this time, nothing in Joseph's bearing that read as calculation at all. "Most who ask me for such a thing ask it for a kinsman, or for a debt owed to a patron still living who might repay the favor. You are neither kin nor debtor to this man, so far as anything reaches my desk."

"No, Prefect. I am neither." Joseph held his gaze without flinching from the plainness of the question. "I ask it because I do not believe, whatever the council has decided among itself, that the man deserved the ground the law would otherwise give him, and because I find I would rather carry the cost of that belief openly than let it sit in me unspoken for the rest of my life, the way I let last night's vote sit in me unspoken while it was still being taken."

Pontius weighed that answer longer than its brevity strictly warranted, hearing in it an echo of something he could not immediately place, and granted the petition before he had fully finished placing it, a decision that cost him, as he had told Claudia the night before, almost nothing to give and that he suspected, watching Joseph's face ease at the granting of it, meant considerably more to the man receiving it than any of the morning's larger business had meant to anyone standing near the tribunal.

## What the Centurion Confirmed

He sent for the centurion who had commanded the detail at the hill before he signed the order releasing the body, a small procedural caution that had nothing to do with Joseph's honesty and everything to do with the habit of six years never releasing a thing from his office without first confirming, on a soldier's word rather than a petitioner's, that the thing being released was exactly what it claimed to be.

"Dead, Prefect, and dead sooner than the other two, by a fair margin." The centurion delivered it in the flat register soldiers used for a report they had learned, across long service, not to dress in any feeling of their own. "One of the men put a spear to his side to be certain, since the Sabbath's approach made haste of the matter for all three, and there was no doubt left in it after that. I would not have released the body on a councillor's word alone, Prefect, whatever his standing. I release it easily enough on my own man's confirmation."

"You seem troubled by the haste of it, Centurion, if I may say so," Pontius said, watching the man a moment longer than the report required. "I have not known you to volunteer more of an account than a question strictly asked for."

"I am not troubled by the haste, Prefect. The Sabbath makes haste of every crucifixion this city ever asks us to carry out, and I have overseen enough of them to have stopped counting the number some years ago." The centurion held his stance, though something in his face suggested the question had cost him more to answer honestly than his flat delivery let on. "I will say only that the man died faster than any I have watched die on that hill, faster than his own frame should have allowed him to, by every measure my years at this work have taught me to expect. I do not have an explanation for it that satisfies me, Prefect, and I have decided, standing here, that I would rather report the fact plainly and let wiser men account for it than pretend I understand something I do not."

Pontius signed the order with the same economical hand he used for every document that crossed his desk, and sat a moment with the stylus set down, turning the centurion's plain report over in a way he did not immediately want to examine too closely, a soldier's flat confirmation of a fact that should have been, by every measure he had ever governed by, no different from confirming a shipment's weight or a garrison's muster. It was, he told himself, exactly that and nothing more. A man had died on a cross, as men condemned to crosses did, and a soldier had confirmed it, as soldiers were trained to confirm such things, and the confirming of it changed nothing about the fact itself, however long his hand rested on the sealed order before he finally set it aside.

## A Second Request

Caiaphas himself came the following morning, the Sabbath's own restrictions apparently weighed and set aside for whatever business had brought him, and Pontius received him with the same wary attention he had given every audience since the high priest first began, two years before, to spend his visits demonstrating something instead of negotiating it.

"The dead man's followers made certain claims while he lived, Prefect, of rising again after three days, and we would not have his own people steal the body in the night and then spread that same claim as though it had been proven, when it was only arranged." Caiaphas delivered the request in his usual unhurried register, though Pontius thought he heard beneath it, for the first time in two years of these audiences, something closer to genuine unease than to calculation. "We ask only a guard at the tomb, and the council's own seal upon the stone, until the third day has safely passed and the claim can trouble no one further."

Pontius considered the request with the same close attention he gave any petition that arrived dressed as a small matter and might, on closer weighing, prove to be something else entirely. "You are asking a Roman detachment to stand watch over a dead man's grave, against the possibility that a handful of scattered followers, who by every report I have received already fled the city in the night rather than face what happened to their teacher, might return in sufficient numbers to move a sealed stone past armed soldiers." He did not trouble to keep the dryness from his voice. "That is a great deal of Roman manpower to spend guarding against a claim you tell me you do not credit."

"I do not credit it, Prefect. I would still rather spend the manpower than spend the alternative, which is answering for the claim afterward, in a city already inclined this season to believe stranger things of this particular man than it has believed of anyone in my lifetime." Caiaphas held his gaze without apology. "You granted the body to a councillor's private grief yesterday at no cost to yourself. Grant me this at the same small cost, and the matter closes itself entirely, for both our offices, by the fourth day."

Pontius granted it, as he had granted the body, for the same reason he had granted a hundred small requests since Sejanus fell, because it cost him almost nothing and closed a door the council might otherwise leave open for later use against him. He watched Caiaphas withdraw satisfied and could not stop turning the shape of the man's unease over, a weight the request itself did not quite account for. A high priest untroubled by a dead man's claims did not, in Pontius's long experience of reading a room's true business beneath its stated one, spend a Sabbath morning securing a guard against a rumor he considered beneath his notice.

## The Ledger

He gave the remainder of that evening to the dispatch he owed Rome, a plain accounting of a provincial disturbance settled within the bounds of the law and without further unrest, and found the drafting of it came to him with an ease that troubled him more than any difficulty would have.

*A Galilean agitator, claiming a kingship contrary to the Emperor's sole authority, was brought before this office by the Jewish council on charges of sedition*, he wrote, the words arranging themselves into the same flat administrative cadence he had used for every report of his six years in office, the standards crisis, the aqueduct riot, Sejanus's fall, each crisis reduced, in the writing of it, to a shape Rome could file and forget among a hundred other provincial disturbances settled the same season across a hundred other provinces. *Examination found no substantive threat to Roman order beyond the acclamation of a crowd already dispersed to its own satisfaction. Sentence was carried out according to law, with the full cooperation of the local council, and the province remains, at the time of this writing, entirely at peace.*

He read the paragraph back once in the lamplight. It was accurate in every particular a governor's report was required to be accurate in, and entirely empty of anything that had actually happened in that courtyard, the note folded against his chest, the basin's water going the color of nothing, the prisoner's unhurried answer that a kingdom could belong to a different order of things than the one a Roman governor was trained to punish. He did not know how to write that second account into a dispatch Rome would read for evidence of a province's stability. What it had cost the man administering it was not the kind of evidence Rome collected, and turning the thought over, he did not entirely wish to know how. A report that admitted everything a day like this one had actually contained was not a report any governor before him had ever survived writing, and he suspected, sealing the wax over his own, that none ever would.

He thought, setting the stylus down a final time, of the aqueduct's own ledgers, the columns of stone and labor and korbanas silver he had once balanced against a priesthood's outrage and called the balancing itself a kind of proof that a thing had been done rightly. A ledger that balanced had always seemed to him, across six years of governing by exactly that method, its own vindication, a column of figures that either closed cleanly or did not, with no third answer available to a man honest enough to do the sums correctly. This dispatch balanced. Every figure in it closed. Reading it a second time in the lamplight, he realized a ledger's clean closing had never before felt, to him, quite so much like a door being shut on a room he had not finished searching.

## Alone with the Account

Claudia had already retired by the time he finished, and he sat a while longer at the desk with the sealed dispatch before him, the lamp burned low, turning over in his mind the same accounting he had turned over every night since Sejanus fell, what a thing cost against what it purchased, what a governor could afford to feel against what he could afford only to have decided.

He thought of Joseph's plain answer in the audience room, that he would rather carry a cost openly than let it sit in him unspoken. The thought had lodged somewhere six years of his own careful measuring had never built a wall thick enough to keep such things out. He had spent this whole day, he understood, sitting alone with the ledger's flat language still drying on the page before him, converting an unbearable morning into a bearable report, a soldier's confirmation, a councillor's petition, a dispatch Rome would read and set aside, each conversion a small mercy he had granted himself without ever once calling it by that name.

He thought, one final time before he let the lamp gutter out, of the centurion's plain report that the man had died faster than the work should have allowed, a fact offered without explanation because the soldier delivering it had none to offer. That detail sat beside Joseph's honesty now, neither the ledger nor six years of practiced measurement able to dislodge either one. He had built his whole theory of governing on the conviction that every fact, weighed carefully enough, eventually explained itself. He sat now with two facts, a councillor's unasked-for honesty and a soldier's unexplained haste, that refused to resolve into anything his own careful weighing knew how to close. He could not decide whether the refusal troubled him more for what it said about the day just past, or for what it said about the limits of a method he had trusted, without serious question, for the whole of his time in this office.

He banked the lamp at last and rose from the desk, the sealed dispatch waiting for morning's courier, and told himself, climbing the stair toward the room where Claudia slept, that he had governed this day exactly as the office required a governor to govern it, evenly, according to the law, without a single decision he could not defend before any tribunal that might one day ask him to account for it. He believed this most of the way up the stair. He believed it a little less by the time he reached the door, and stood there a moment in the dark, listening to his wife's slow breathing on the other side of it, before he let himself in to whatever sleep the night still owed him.
