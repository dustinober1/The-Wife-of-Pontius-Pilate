# Chapter 26: Rumors of an Empty Tomb

## The Third Day

The report reached the palace on the morning of the third day, carried first by Rufus in the flat, procedural register he used for anything that had not yet resolved itself into a settled account, and Claudia heard it from the doorway of Pontius's study before either man noticed she had come to stand there.

"The guard at the tomb sends word the stone has been moved, Prefect, and the body is no longer inside it. They cannot agree among themselves on how it happened. Two of them swear they were struck senseless by something none of them can describe plainly, and woke to find the seal broken and the tomb empty. The others say only that they slept, which is the account that will cost them their lives if it stands as the official one, and so I do not expect it to stand as the official one for very much longer."

Pontius received this with the same weighing stillness Claudia had watched him bring to every crisis of the past two years, though she thought she saw, beneath the stillness, something closer to genuine irritation than to alarm. "A guard that sleeps at its post, or a guard that invents a vision to excuse having slept at its post. Those are the only two accounts a rational man needs to weigh here, Rufus, whatever a frightened soldier tells you in the dark two hours after the fact."

"Caiaphas's people are already offering the soldiers a considerable sum, Prefect, to swear to the sleeping account rather than the other, and to let it be known besides that the dead man's own followers stole the body while the guard slept, to make good on a claim he made in life." Rufus delivered this without inflection, though Claudia, watching from the doorway, thought she saw in his face the same discomfort she had heard in his voice reporting the standards crisis years before, a soldier uneasy at being asked to help manage a story rather than a fact. "It is, I think, the account most convenient to everyone involved, Prefect, which does not make it the true one, only the one most likely to be believed."

## What Pontius Made of It

"Then let them pay for it, and let the story stand." Pontius set down the report with the same economy he brought to every closed matter, and Claudia understood, watching him, that he had already filed the whole affair away in the same part of his mind where he kept every provincial disturbance too small to trouble Rome with a second dispatch. "A missing body is a theft, Claudia, whatever poetry his followers dress it in afterward. Grief invents visions. A guard invents excuses. Neither invention requires anything more mysterious to explain it than a body already missing before the theft's own convenient story caught up to account for the missing."

"You do not find it strange. That men trained to guard a tomb would rather confess a capital offense than simply say they saw nothing unusual at all."

"I find soldiers say a great many strange things in the hour after they have failed at the one task given them, Claudia, and I have learned, across six years commanding men in this province, not to build a theory of the world on the first excuse a frightened soldier offers me before he has had time to settle on a better one." He said it without unkindness, though she heard in it the same flat certainty he had used, two years before, to dismiss Herod's fear of a resurrected John as a tetrarch's guilty nerves rather than a fact worth building a policy on. "The council will manage its own story. I have a province to govern that has nothing further to do with one dead man's tomb, whatever his followers now choose to make of an empty one."

She did not press him further, understanding, from long practice, the particular register in which he had just closed a subject rather than merely paused it, and left him to his correspondence with the same unsettled feeling she had carried since the basin's water first refused to resolve itself into an answer she trusted.

She thought, walking back through the colonnade, of every crisis she had watched him close in exactly this manner across six years of marriage, the standards protest reduced to a policy decided and never revisited, the aqueduct riot's dead folded into a report that named a number rather than a name, Sejanus's fall converted, within a season, into a lesson about patrons rather than a grief about a man. Pontius had always governed by closing doors cleanly behind him, and she had always understood the closing as a kind of strength, a governor's necessary economy in a province that offered him a fresh crisis every season whether he had finished mourning the last one or not. She found herself wondering, for the first time in six years of admiring that same economy, whether a door closed too cleanly was still, underneath the clean closing, a door that had simply never been opened wide enough to see what waited on the other side of it.

## What Tamar Had Heard by a Different Road

Tamar came to her that same afternoon with a report that had traveled by no official channel at all, and told it to her with a carefulness Claudia recognized as the same reserve she had worn discussing John before his death and the teacher before his.

"Some of the women who followed him, Domina, went to the tomb at first light to complete the burial rites his own hasty entombment before the Sabbath had left unfinished, and found the stone already moved and the body already gone." Tamar chose her words with visible care, watching Claudia's face as she offered each one. "They say they were told, by someone at the tomb, that he was not there because he had risen, exactly as he had said he would, before any of this began. I do not know who told them this, Domina, or in what form, because the account itself grows stranger with each teller between the tomb and this kitchen. My own cousin's wife tells it with a young man in white seated where the body should have lain. Her own sister-in-law, who was among the women themselves, tells it with two such figures, and neither woman will yield the point to the other, though both agree entirely on everything that matters more than the counting. My cousin's wife swears her sister-in-law has not stopped weeping since, though she says now the weeping is not grief."

"And do you believe it."

"I do not know what I believe, Domina, any more than I have known it at any point since this began. I know only that a story invented to cover a theft is usually a simpler story than the one growing through the market today, and that the women telling it have nothing obvious to gain from inventing something so difficult for anyone, including themselves, to credit." Tamar held Claudia's gaze a long moment. "I have watched hope die once already in this city, when John's head came back on a platter and none of us were left with anything but the platter to explain. I do not yet know what to call a hope that instead comes back with an empty tomb behind it. I only know it does not feel, this time, like the kind of hope I have learned to expect will disappoint me."

Marcus, passing through the room with the evening's lamps, paused long enough to add the one detail he had gathered on his own account, delivered in the careful register he used when he judged a thing worth saying even though no one had asked it of him. "The guards' story is already the one being told in the garrison mess tonight, Domina, the sleeping and the bribe both, and told with a great deal of laughing at the men who nearly signed their own death warrants with the truth before the council's silver reached them. I do not think a single man in that mess believes the story they are laughing at. I think they are laughing because a soldier who does not know what else to do with a thing he cannot explain finds it easier to laugh at the cover story than to sit with the fact the cover story was invented to hide."

## What Claudia Could Not Set Down

She sat with a fresh page before her that evening, in the same private practice she had kept since the letter she burned after the colonnade riot, and found, laying the reed pen against it, that she could not make herself write the words she had written twice before, *I do not believe in omens*, because she no longer believed, turning the sentence over in her mind before committing it to the page, that the sentence was true.

She wrote instead, slowly, testing each word before she let the next follow it: *I watched a basin of water fail to answer a question. I watched a sky change color at midday and return to itself by evening as though nothing had happened. I have been told, by a woman I trust more than I trust most Roman reports that reach my husband's desk, that a tomb sealed by Roman authority and guarded by Roman soldiers no longer holds what it was sealed to hold. I do not know what any of this means. I no longer believe that not knowing what it means excuses me from attending to it.*

She read the page back once, and did not burn it. She held it a long while over the lamp's flame, close enough that the heat reached her fingers, and found, for the first time since she had begun this private practice years before, that she could not bring herself to let the words blacken past reading. She rolled it instead, small and tight, and placed it in the same chest where she kept the few things from this marriage she had judged worth keeping past their season, the brass key from her own garden gate in Rome, a length of ribbon from a wedding she barely remembered as a girl of seventeen, and understood, closing the lid over it, that she had just done something she could not undo, and did not, this time, want to.
