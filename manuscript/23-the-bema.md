# Chapter 23: The Bema

## The View from the Balcony

She had lived this instant already, in fragments spread across three nights of dreaming, and she came out onto the terrace now with no more warning than the dream had ever given her, a palla thrown over her sleeping tunic, her bare feet slapping cold mosaic underfoot.

The court below was already exactly as she had feared, and worse for being real. Roman soldiers held a line against a crowd whose noise carried no single voice left in it, only the massed and rising temper of men who had decided something and meant to see it carried through before the day's heat set in fully. Across from them stood the accusers, temple officials gathering their fine robes as they pressed their case, and between the two lines, bound with coarse rope, stood the man from her dream, exactly as still as he had always been in it, exactly as unbearable to look at directly.

Pontius was climbing the tribunal steps with his back to her, and she knew, watching the set of his shoulders, that whatever composure had carried him through the standards crisis and the colonnade's aftermath, he had brought here too, and that it was not going to be enough. He believed this a hearing he could still measure his way through. He could not see, from where he stood, the trap the morning had already closed around him.

## The Summons and the Message

She was moving before she had finished the thought, back through the tapestries into the reception room, calling for Marcus before she had crossed half the distance to her writing table.

He came at a run, the small ink-stain on his thumb the first ordinary thing her eye caught in a room gone suddenly unsteady. "Mistress, what is wrong?"

"There is no time," she said, already reaching for parchment and a reed pen, her hand shaking hard enough that the first stroke of ink bled wide across the grain. "Write it exactly as I say it, and do not stop to ask me why."

He dipped the pen. She dictated it in the same words the dream had left branded into her across three nights: *Have nothing to do with that innocent man, for I have suffered much today because of a dream.* She rolled the scrap tight before the ink had fully set and pressed it into his hands.

"Not to a captain. Not to a guard. His own scribe, at the foot of the dais, and into no other hands but his."

Marcus was gone before she finished speaking, and she stood alone a moment in the emptied room, her own fingers already inked from the haste of the writing, understanding only that she could not have stood at that rail one moment longer without having done something, whether or not the something changed anything at all.

## The Knife-Edge Moment

She returned to the terrace in time to watch him make the crossing, a slight figure threading the court's edge, keeping close to the wall the way a boy learns to keep close to a wall when he does not want to be noticed by men with swords. Every rule of the household she had been raised to keep told her she should not stand exposed at that rail, a governor's wife visible to a mob at the exact hour her husband most needed to look strong to it. She stayed anyway.

Marcus reached the dais and pressed the scroll into the scribe's hands rather than risk the steps himself, then fell back to the crowd's edge to watch what became of it. The scribe carried it up. Pontius took it without looking up, still mid-sentence, and she watched, across the whole width of the court, the exact moment the words reached him: his jaw slackening, his breath catching hard enough to move the bronze clasp at his collar, the irritation in his face going hollow.

He lifted his head at last, and found her across the forty yards of dust and noise separating the terrace from the tribunal, and held her gaze the way he had held it only a handful of times in the whole of their marriage, as though the answer to what came next belonged to both of them and to neither, the prisoner standing between them and the whole weight of what followed resting, for one suspended moment, on nothing more than the two of them looking at each other across a distance neither could cross.

## What Rufus Carried Up

He looked away from her first, folding the note once and setting it inside the fold of his toga rather than discarding it, a small gesture Claudia read from her distance as the only answer he had time to give her before the crowd's noise demanded the rest of him back. He turned to the prisoner and said something she could not hear at that distance, and the prisoner answered him, unhurried, in words that carried no farther than the front rank of the accusers, and whatever passed between them seemed, from the set of Pontius's shoulders, to unsettle him more than any threat the crowd itself had yet made.

Rufus came up to the terrace twice in the hour that followed, each time briefly, each time leaving her with fragments she pieced together the way she had once pieced together a dispatch's meaning from Philotas's careful summaries. "He asked the prisoner plainly whether he claimed to be a king, Domina, and the man answered him in a manner that was no denial, though it was not quite a claim either, something closer to saying the word itself belonged to a different order of things than the one a Roman governor was trained to punish. The Prefect came away from it saying to Rufus privately that he found no charge in the man worth a Roman sentence, whatever the council wished to name it."

The sun climbed higher over the pavement as she waited, and the heat pooling in the courtyard seemed to Claudia to thicken the crowd's noise instead of thinning it, the way a closed room only grew louder, never quieter, as more bodies pressed into it. She gripped the balustrade with both hands now, no longer trusting her own legs to hold her upright unassisted through whatever the morning still meant to ask of her. Marcus stood at her shoulder through most of it, having returned once his errand was finished and finding, she suspected, no better use for himself than to stand near her the way he had stood near every crisis of this household's six years in the province, present without needing to be asked, his jaw doing its old tightening thing each time the crowd's noise swelled toward something neither of them yet had a name for.

"Then why does the shouting continue."

"Because the council will not accept a governor's private finding, Domina, only a public verdict, and the crowd the council has gathered presses harder with each hour that passes without one." Rufus glanced back over the balustrade before he continued. "The Prefect sent him briefly to Herod, since the man is a Galilean and Herod happened to be in the city for the feast, hoping the tetrarch's own jurisdiction might relieve him of the judgment entirely. Herod sent him back within the hour, no wiser for the questioning and no more willing than the Prefect to pronounce a sentence himself. I am told the two men parted on friendlier terms than they have managed in years, having found, in this one matter at least, that neither wished to own the deciding of it."

"And Herod said nothing of use to him. Nothing that might have settled it."

"Herod asked him to perform some sign for his own amusement, Domina, by what the guards who attended them both tell it, and the man gave him nothing, not a word, not a gesture, the whole of the audience. Herod's own soldiers mocked him for it before sending him back, a purple robe thrown over his shoulders as a jest at the crowd's own naming of him, but the Prefect tells Rufus that even the mockery seemed, to anyone watching closely, to land nowhere the man himself was standing." Rufus delivered this with the same flat competence he gave every report, though Claudia thought she heard, beneath it, something closer to genuine unsettlement than professional distance usually allowed him to show.

## Barabbas

She lost sight of Pontius again when he withdrew once more into the shade of the portico, and it was the crowd itself that told her, by its shifting temper, when he returned to the bema: a surge forward, a name shouted in a register she recognized from the standards protest as something beyond ordinary grievance, an insistence that had stopped asking and begun demanding.

Marcus, returned to her side by then, his errand finished and his usefulness for the moment spent, named it for her outright. "It is the custom at the feast, Domina, for the governor to release one prisoner the crowd chooses, and the council has put forward a man called Barabbas, held for insurrection and for a killing in the disturbance that earned it, against the teacher, meaning to force a choice they already know the outcome of before it is asked."

She watched the shape of the choice play out below without being able to hear a word of it, Pontius's arm lifted toward the bound prisoner and then toward another man brought forward from the guardroom, haggard and blinking in the light, and the crowd's answer arriving as a single roar she did not need Marcus to translate for her. Pontius's shoulders, even at that distance, told her the rest: a man who had found no charge worth a sentence, being handed, regardless, the sentence to give.

"He tried once more, Domina, before he gave the order." Marcus said it quietly, watching the same scene she watched, his hands closed around the balustrade's stone edge with a tightness she had not seen in him since the week John died. "Rufus tells me he asked the crowd plainly what evil the man had done, and the only answer that came back to him was the same name, louder, until asking again would only have cost him whatever authority he still had left in that courtyard to ask anything of them at all. I do not think, Domina, that a governor who has already lost the crowd's patience has very much choice left in a matter like this one, whatever choice the law still says belongs to him alone."

She thought of Pontius's own words to her, the night the Sejanus dispatch reached the harbor, that a governor who governed by fear reasoned by a logic with no fixed terms, only the shifting question of what any given silence might cost him. Here, watching him give the order that sent one bound man free and kept the other standing where he was, was the fullest and most public proof yet of exactly what that fear, left to run its course two more years, had finally made of him.

## The Water

It was the basin that told her he had decided, before Rufus climbed the terrace stairs a final time to confirm it. A servant carried it out to the tribunal's edge at Pontius's own gesture, and Pontius washed his hands over it before the whole court, slowly, in a manner too deliberate to be anything but a message aimed at every eye still watching him, Roman and Jewish alike.

"He says he is innocent of the man's blood, Domina, and that the charge is theirs to answer for, not his." Rufus delivered it without embellishment, the way he delivered every report he judged too heavy to carry any ornament of his own. "Caiaphas and the council with him answered that they would carry the charge themselves. He has ordered the flogging carried out, and the sentence after it, in the manner the law provides for a man condemned of claiming a kingship not his own to claim."

Claudia stood at the balustrade a long while after Rufus withdrew, watching the crowd below begin its slow, satisfied unknotting, the accusers already turning back toward the Temple with the loose-shouldered ease of men whose morning's business had concluded to their liking, the soldiers reforming around the prisoner to lead him from the pavement toward whatever waited for him beyond the gate. She found she could not look away from the basin itself, still standing at the tribunal's edge where the servant had left it, the water inside it gone the color of nothing in particular in the hard morning light, and thought, watching it, of a fountain in a Roman garden years before, of black water at Ostia the night she first understood how far this life would carry her from everything she had known, and could not have said, watching this newest water in its plain clay basin, whether it washed anything clean at all or only made a private ceremony of a decision already made before the water was ever called for.

Pontius came to her an hour later, once the court below had emptied and the soldiers had marched their prisoner out toward the place the law required such sentences to be carried out, and stood beside her at the balustrade without speaking for some time, the bronze clasp at his collar still catching the light the way it had when the note first reached him.

"You tried to stop me." He said it without accusation, in a voice she had not heard him use before, stripped of the flat administrative register he brought to every crisis of the past two years. "I have never known you to send me a warning in the middle of a public tribunal. I do not think, whatever else this day has cost either of us, that I will forget the sight of you standing here when I looked up."

"Did the dream mean anything to you."

"I do not know what I believe it meant." He looked out over the emptying pavement, the basin still standing where the servant had left it, and for a long moment neither of them said anything further, the morning's heat already climbing toward the full weight of a Jerusalem noon, the city beyond the walls carrying on about its feast as though nothing in this one courtyard had just altered the whole of what either of them would carry forward from it. "I know only that I have spent six years believing a governor's hardest decisions were the ones he could measure. I do not think I will ever again mistake that belief for the whole of what governing actually costs a man who holds the water and the sentence both in the same two hands."

"You said, that night the Sejanus dispatch came, that you did not think you would roll the map out again soon. I do not think I have seen you unroll it once since." She said it without accusation, watching him rather than the pavement now. "What replaces a map, Pontius, for a man who has stopped trusting what it shows him."

"I do not know yet. Perhaps nothing does. Perhaps a man simply governs afterward with his eyes open to how little the measuring ever actually protected him from, and calls that the whole of his remaining wisdom." He turned from the balustrade at last, and she saw in his face, in the slack unguarded line of his jaw, something she had not seen there since the first meeting he had once described to her himself, an honesty that asked nothing of the moment except to be allowed to exist in it a while longer before the office reclaimed him. "I gave the order, Claudia. Whatever you saw in your dream, whatever it meant or did not mean, I gave the order regardless, with your warning already folded against my chest where I could feel the shape of it the whole time I was giving it. I do not know what that makes me. I only know I will carry the knowing of it a good deal longer than I carried anything Sejanus's fall ever cost me."

She had no comfort to offer him, and did not pretend one, and stood beside him instead at the balustrade as the basin's water sat undisturbed in the climbing heat and the city beyond the walls went on, indifferent and enormous, toward whatever the rest of this Passover still meant to make of the morning neither of them would ever fully set down again.
