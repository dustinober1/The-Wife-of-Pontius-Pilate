# Chapter 13: The Baptist's Head

## What Came from Machaerus

Nearly a year had passed since the visit to Tiberias, a year in which the grazing dispute had settled into a durable if uneasy peace between the two territories and Claudia had heard nothing further of the prisoner at Machaerus beyond the occasional secondhand fragment: that he still lived, that Antipas still visited him some nights, that the crowds along the Jordan had thinned but not vanished, holding to whatever hope they had carried away from the river before the man who gave it to them was taken out of their reach. She had almost let herself believe, in the ordinary business of a household running smoothly through another Judaean summer, that the fortress might simply hold him indefinitely, a problem Antipas preferred unresolved to resolved, the way a man leaves a difficult letter unanswered on his desk, unwilling to commit to whichever answer the reply will require of him.

The news reached Caesarea the way most news from Antipas's territory reached them, secondhand and already reshaped by every mouth it had passed through on the way, so that by the time Tamar carried it into the peristyle garden where Claudia sat with the household accounts spread across her lap, it had already hardened into the kind of story that would outlive every plainer version of the same events.

"They are saying the tetrarch gave a birthday feast," Tamar said, setting a jar of oil down harder than the errand required, a habit Claudia had come to recognize by now as the shape grief took in her before the grief itself found words. "That his wife's daughter danced for the guests, and pleased him well enough that he swore an oath in front of the whole table to give her whatever she asked, and that the girl went out and asked her mother what she should ask for, and her mother told her." Tamar's hands had gone still over the oil jar, though her voice had not. "The head of the man at Machaerus. Brought in on a platter while the guests were still at table, so that no one in the hall could pretend afterward they had not seen it."

Claudia set the household ledger aside with a care that felt, even to her, like a woman handling something fragile mostly to give her hands an occupation while the rest of her caught up to what had just been said. She thought of the colonnade at Tiberias, the torches on the dark water, Salome drawing her knees up beneath her with a girl's unfinished composure, saying *I do not yet know what winning will look like, when it comes, or what she will ask of the rest of us to help her get there.* She had not imagined, hearing it, that the answer would arrive this quickly, or in this shape, or that the girl who had spoken so carefully of a man she had never seen would be the one asked to carry the winning out of the banquet hall on a plate.

"Is it true," Claudia asked, "or is it the story the crowds have decided to tell because the story is better than whatever actually happened in that fortress?"

"I do not know that it matters, Domina, which one it is." Tamar's voice had gone flat in the particular way it went flat when a thing had moved past argument into simple fact she expected no one to contest. "He is dead either way. The tetrarch had him killed either from a rash oath at a birthday table, or because a man with that many followers frightened him too much to let go on living, and I have heard both reasons offered by different mouths in the same market this morning, and neither reason makes the man any less dead than the other would."

She was quiet a moment, turning the oil jar in her hands without setting it down, and when she spoke again her voice had sharpened into something Claudia had rarely heard turned on her directly. "Your husband will call it politics solved, when the report reaches him. One voice fewer troubling a tetrarch's sleep. No Roman hand did the killing." Her grip tightened on the jar. "You stay clean, Domina, by letting the local kings do it for you."

"That is not entirely fair," Claudia said, more startled by it than she let show. "You do not yet know what he will say of it."

"No. I do not." Tamar picked the jar back up, though her hands were not yet steady enough to be trusted with it. "I do not have the patience this morning to be fair first and honest after. Forgive me, Domina, if you can. I cannot forgive myself the same today."

She did not wait for an answer, and Claudia let her go, understanding that whatever she might have said in her own husband's defense would have cost Tamar something to hear that this morning's grief had left her no room to spend.

Claudia sat with the ledger closed in her lap for a long while after Tamar had gone back inside, watching the household's ordinary afternoon continue around her, a maid crossing the far end of the garden with a basket of washing, cicadas working steadily in the heat where conversation had been, and found herself unable to settle the news into any of the categories a piece of political intelligence usually settled into by now, three years into learning how this province absorbed its griefs and moved past them. A riot she had learned, however painfully, to hold at a fixed distance in memory, a thing that had happened and could be accounted for, mourned, carried forward as a private practice of small coins sent through Tamar's hands. This felt different in a way she could not yet name, closer to a personal loss than the scale of her actual acquaintance with the man justified, as though some part of her had gone on, without her permission, expecting the story at Tiberias to end differently than it had.

## Simon

She found Simon in the kitchen court that evening, alone at the hour when the rest of the staff had already gone to their meal, sitting on an overturned crate with a knife and a basket of unpeeled roots in front of him that he had not touched in what looked, from the small drift of peelings beside the untouched basket, like a considerable while.

"You have heard," she said, and it was not a question, and he did not treat it as one.

"My uncle heard it first, from a man who had it from a soldier at Machaerus itself, or claimed to." Simon did not look up from the knife turning slowly in his hands. "I went into the water with him, Domina. You remember. I told you how it felt after. He said the water was only the outward sign. That the real thing had to happen somewhere else first. Somewhere the water couldn't reach." He set the knife down at last, carefully, the way a man sets down something he has only just realized he might otherwise throw. "I keep thinking of his voice. Telling us that." He stopped, then started again. "And then there's the platter. The birthday feast." He shook his head. "I don't know how both things fit in the same world. I don't know how they fit in me."

"I do not think they are meant to sit together," Claudia said, and found, saying it, that she believed it more than she had expected to when she began the sentence. "I think that is precisely the horror of it. That a man can say a true thing in a river and be right about it, and a woman can dance at a table forty miles away and be granted a head for it, and the world goes on holding both of those at once without ever once making them make sense beside each other."

"You met her. The girl who danced." Simon looked up at her for the first time since she had come into the court, something searching in the look that she understood only slowly was not accusation but a plain question, a boy wanting to know whether the woman who had eaten at that household's table had seen in the daughter anything that might explain what the daughter had gone on to ask for. "What was she like?"

Claudia thought of the colonnade at Tiberias, the lake beyond it too dark to show its own bottom, a girl not much older than Simon himself drawing her knees up and admitting, in the one unguarded conversation of the whole evening, that she did not yet know what her mother's winning would look like when it finally arrived. "She did not seem to me a girl who wanted a man's head," Claudia said carefully, choosing the honesty of it over whatever comfort a simpler answer might have offered him. "She seemed to me a girl caught between people more certain than she was, asked to carry out a request she may not have fully understood the weight of until the platter was already in her hands. I do not say that to excuse her. I say it because I think the excuse and the accusation are both too simple for what I actually saw that night, and I would rather tell you the complicated truth than a clean lie in either direction."

Simon was quiet long enough that she thought he might not answer at all, and when he did, his voice had lost the careful evenness he usually kept in front of the household. "He told us, at the river, that someone greater was coming. That he was only clearing the road, the way a man clears stones before a cart." Simon turned the knife over again. "I keep wondering if it's heard yet. That the one who cleared the road is dead." He stopped. "Whether it still comes now. Now that there's no one left to announce it." He picked the knife back up, turned it once more in the lamplight, and returned, finally, to the roots in the basket, a boy going back to work because the work was the only shape left that the evening would still let him hold. Claudia left him to it, understanding that whatever comfort she might offer him belonged to a language she had not yet learned to speak fluently enough to risk it.

## Pontius's Accounting

She told Pontius that night, over a supper neither of them ate much of, and watched him receive the news the way he received every report that reached his desk from beyond his own province's borders: with interest, with a certain professional attention to the mechanics of it, and with no discernible weight attached to the fact that a man was dead who had, three years earlier, drawn half of Galilee to a river to hear him speak.

"Antipas has solved a problem he should have solved a year ago," Pontius said, turning his cup slowly on the table in the gesture she had learned meant he was working through a political calculation, not a feeling. "A prisoner unsilenced but unexecuted invites exactly the kind of pressure he has apparently been living under at Machaerus, his own household pressing him from one side and the crowds who followed the man pressing him, in absence, from the other. He has removed the pressure from both directions in a single stroke, however clumsily the occasion presented itself. I would not have chosen a birthday oath as the instrument. But the outcome is not, from where I sit, difficult to understand."

"That is all it is to you. An outcome."

"What would you have it be?" He said it without heat, genuinely asking rather than deflecting, which she had learned by now was somehow worse than if he had simply dismissed her. "I did not know the man. I have never set foot in his tetrarchy. I have a province of my own with its own dead to account for, and I do not have the room in me, Claudia, to carry the full weight of every death that happens within a hundred miles of my border as though it were my own to grieve."

"I am not asking you to grieve him. I am asking you to notice that you have not." She heard, saying it, an echo of the conversation on the road back from Tiberias, the same shape of accusation dressed now in a starker case, and pressed on before she could soften it. "Herodias told me herself, at that dinner, that her husband could not decide which frightened him more, the crowd outside his walls or the prisoner inside them. He has decided now. He chose the crowd, and paid for the choice with a man's head on a platter, and you call it a problem solved rather than a man murdered, and I do not know how to make you hear the difference between those two accounts of the same fact when I am not entirely certain, watching you tonight, that there is a difference left in you to hear it with."

He set his cup down with the deliberate click she had learned to recognize as the sound of a man absorbing a blow he did not intend to let show on his face, and for a moment she thought he might answer her in kind, matching accusation with accusation the way their worst arguments had sometimes gone in the first years of the marriage. Instead he was quiet, turning the cup once more, and when he spoke his voice had none of the flat register he had used across the whole of the meal.

"Perhaps there is not," he said. "I have spent three years in this province learning to look at a death and ask first what it costs me to answer for it, and I do not know any longer how to unlearn that quickly enough to grieve a stranger the way you seem to think I should. I am not certain that frightens me less than it should frighten you." He rose from the table before she could answer, leaving his supper unfinished, and she sat alone in the lamplight a long while afterward, turning his last sentence over the way he turned his figures, and found she could not decide whether it was the most honest thing he had said to her all year, or the most despairing, or whether, by now, those two things had become for him one and the same.

She went to find Simon again before she retired that night, not to say anything further to him, only to see that the roots had been peeled and put away and the kitchen court left as it should be left, a small unremarkable check she told herself was owed to any member of the household who had spent an evening grieving alone, and told herself less convincingly was not also owed to some private need of her own to confirm that grief, once let into a house, could still be tended, and not simply endured. He was long since gone to his sleeping mat by then, the basket empty, the knife hung back on its peg, and she stood a moment in the dark kitchen court doing nothing useful at all, before returning to her own rooms to lie awake beside a husband who had not come to bed, listening to the house settle around her the way it settled every night, indifferent to what had been decided at a birthday table forty miles away, indifferent to a boy's grief over a river he had once waded into believing it would change something, indifferent, she thought, to nearly everything except the ledgers both she and her husband kept, each in their own way, to prove to themselves they had been of use to somebody before the account closed.
