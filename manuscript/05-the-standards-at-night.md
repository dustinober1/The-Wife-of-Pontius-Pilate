# Chapter 5: The Standards at Night

## News from Jerusalem

Claudia heard of it first from Philotas, who came to her rooms an hour after dawn with the particular stillness of a man who has decided, before he speaks, exactly how little of his own opinion he intends to let show.

"The garrison marched into Jerusalem last night, Domina. The winter cohort, from here to the Antonia. The Prefect ordered it done after dark, the standards wrapped in cloth until they were inside the fortress walls." He paused, folding his hands together in a way she had come to recognize as his substitute for a longer sentence he had judged unwise to finish. "They were unwrapped this morning, on the Prefect's order, so the garrison might display them properly from the fortress towers, in view of the Temple courts."

"Wrapped." Claudia set down the cup she had been holding, untasted, since he entered. "He hid them to get them into the city, and then uncovered them once they were already inside the walls, where no one could stop him without storming a Roman fortress to do it."

"I could not say what the Prefect intended, Domina."

"You could say it. You've simply decided not to, in front of me." She said it without unkindness, and Philotas's mouth tightened in a way that told her she had guessed correctly and that he would rather have died than confirm it aloud. She let him go without pressing further. She had learned, in three months in this country, that the household staff carried opinions about her husband's decisions the way Tamar carried opinions about standing water: fully formed, carefully hidden, and offered only when the asking came from someone they had already decided to trust.

She found Pontius in the audience hall, bent over a dispatch with the settled satisfaction of a man who believes a difficult thing has already been accomplished, not merely begun.

"You moved the garrison," she said.

"I moved a garrison that has wintered in Caesarea for eleven years into the fortress that overlooks the city it is meant to police. It is not a provocation, Claudia. It is a competent disposition of forces, the kind any governor before me should have made years ago."

"You told their elders, in this room, that the standards would stay out of the city."

"I told them the standards would stay out of the Temple courts." He did not look up from the dispatch. "I have kept that word exactly. The fortress is not the Temple. The towers overlook the Temple, which is a different matter entirely, and one I explained to them with perfect clarity."

"You explained a distinction they did not ask for and will not accept, and you made certain they would learn of it only after there was nothing left for them to do about it." She came to stand across the table from him, close enough that he finally raised his eyes to hers. "Pontius, you did this at night. Wrapped in cloth, like something smuggled. A man does not hide an action he believes is just. He hides an action he already knows will be received as an injury."

Something crossed his face, quick and defensive, the same expression she remembered from the study in Rome the night before they sailed, the look of a man absorbing a true thing faster than his pride could find a way to dismiss it. "I hid it to avoid exactly the disorder that open movement would have caused. A cohort marching through Jerusalem's streets in daylight, standards uncovered, inviting every idle hand in the city to throw a stone at a soldier who cannot strike back without starting the very riot I mean to prevent. I judged the quiet approach the more merciful one."

"Merciful to your soldiers. Not to the people who will wake tomorrow and find the Emperor's image already fixed above their Temple, with no warning given and no chance to object before the thing was already finished."

He set the dispatch down, and for a moment the old argument from the study in Rome seemed to hang in the room between them again, unfinished business finally come due. "The thing is finished, Claudia. Whatever they feel about how it was done, it is done, and it will remain done. I did not come to this province to negotiate the Emperor's image out of his own garrisons."

She did not answer that, because she understood, watching him return to the dispatch with the air of a man closing a ledger, that there was nothing left in the argument he was willing to hear. She left him to his papers and went instead to find Marcus, who told her, in the flat careful voice he used for news he suspected she would not welcome, that riders had already left Jerusalem for Caesarea before the standards were fully unwrapped from their cloth, carrying word of the thing to every town between.

## The Petitioners

They arrived on the fourth day, not as a mob but as something more disciplined and, Claudia thought, watching them assemble in the open ground before the praetorium's outer gate, considerably harder to answer. Men in the hundreds, then more still coming in by the road from the south, sitting down in the dust in loose ranks, some with women and children beside them, none of them armed, none of them shouting, all of them simply present, in numbers that grew through the day until the ground before the gate had the look of a small city encamped on principle alone.

Tamar found her at the courtyard wall that overlooked the gathering, and stood beside her a long moment without speaking, the two of them watching the crowd settle into the patient postures of a vigil.

"They will not leave," Tamar said finally. "I should tell you that directly, Domina, before your husband's officers convince him otherwise. I know some of these men. My own cousin is among them, three rows back from the gate, the one with the gray shawl. They have not come to shout and go home by evening. They have come to sit until the standards are removed or until something worse happens to them than sitting."

"How long can they sit?"

"As long as it takes. Longer than your husband will find comfortable, I expect." Tamar's eyes did not leave the crowd. "There is a kind of patience your people don't have a word for, Domina, because you have never needed it. We have buried it into the law itself. A man can fast, can wait, can hold a single position for days if the thing he is holding it for is worth more to him than his own comfort. Your husband understands force. He has not yet learned to answer patience, because patience does not attack, and he has no order that tells him how to strike back at a man who is only sitting still."

Claudia watched a woman near the front pass a skin of water down the line, saw children being settled into shade improvised from cloaks stretched between staves, saw the whole slow architecture of a people preparing to be uncomfortable for as long as necessary rather than for as long as convenient. She thought of the map unrolled on the study table in Rome, the fine black ink, the wide blank margins where she had once asked her husband where the sacred boundaries lay, and understood, watching the crowd settle for what might be its first night in the open ground, that she was looking at one of the boundaries the cartographer had left unmarked, because no measuring rod existed that could put a number to it.

Marcus came to stand at her other side as the light failed, his wax tablet for once left somewhere else, his hands empty and folded, the posture of a man who has run out of inventory to take and is simply watching, as she was, because there was nothing left to do but that.

"The Prefect has ordered the gates held shut," Marcus said quietly. "No provisions in or out through the main court. He says he means only to discourage them, not to starve them."

"And if they do not discourage?"

Marcus did not answer, and his silence told her more clearly than any account from Philotas could have, that the household itself, staff and steward alike, had already begun to wonder the same thing, and had not yet found an answer that let anyone sleep easily.

## Five Days

The crowd was still there on the fifth morning, thinner in places where illness or exhaustion had sent a few of the old and the very young home, but larger in others, replenished by fresh arrivals from towns further inland who had heard by now what was happening at Caesarea and had come to add their own weight to it. Claudia watched from the wall each day, unable to keep entirely away, and found that the watching itself had become a kind of discipline, an accounting she owed someone, though she could not have said who.

On the third day Pontius had sent soldiers among them, not to strike, only to walk the rows with an officer reading a proclamation that promised nothing and threatened everything in the careful, bloodless language of Roman administration, and the crowd had listened in silence and had not moved. On the fourth day he had sent the same soldiers again, this time armed for something more than reading, and still the crowd had not moved, only bent their heads lower, as though bracing not against blows but against weather.

She found him alone that evening in the room that had become his study, staring at a map of the city he had not troubled to consult in days, his hand resting flat on the parchment instead of tracing it.

"They will not leave," she said, echoing Tamar without meaning to.

"They will leave when they understand that sitting in the dust changes nothing." His voice had lost some of the certainty she remembered from Rome, worn thin by four sleepless nights of officers reporting a standoff that refused to resolve itself by any method he had been trained to apply. "I did not survive fifteen years in the legions to be dictated to by a crowd of farmers who have decided that patience is a weapon."

"Perhaps it is the only weapon they have left you no other way to answer," Claudia said. "You came to this province with a theory, Pontius. An even hand and an unbending spine, you told me, in the study, the night before we sailed. I wonder if you ever considered what a spine does when the thing pressing against it does not strike, and does not tire, and simply waits for the bone to admit whether it was iron or only pride wearing iron's shape."

He did not answer that. He only looked down at the map beneath his hand, at the fine black ink and the wooden eagle he had once set so certainly over the cluster of lines that marked Jerusalem, and said nothing at all, which frightened her more, standing in the doorway of his study on the fifth night of a siege made of nothing but stillness, than any answer he might have given her instead.

Outside, beyond the walls, five days of silence went on gathering itself into something that would have to break, one way or another, before the sixth day came.
