# Chapter 4: The Prefect's House

## The First Morning

Pontius woke before the household did, which he had expected, and lay for a moment listening to a silence that was wrong in ways he could not yet name. No iron-shod wheels on basalt. No river-murmur of the Subura three streets off. Only the sea, working at the base of the promontory with a patience that sounded, to a man raised in a city built on hills, faintly insolent.

He rose and dressed himself, waving off the body-slave who came stumbling in half-asleep, and walked the length of the audience hall alone before the household staff had finished lighting the kitchen fires. Philotas found him there, standing over Herod's mosaic floor with his hands clasped behind his back, studying the pale ghost-shapes in the plaster where some cautious predecessor had ordered the more graven of the old king's birds and beasts scraped away.

"You're an early riser, Prefect." Philotas said it carefully, the way a man tests footing on a bridge he has not yet decided will hold his weight. "Gratus rarely rose before the second hour."

"Gratus governed eleven years and left me tax rolls in three different hands and no index to any of them." Pontius did not look away from the floor. "I intend to know what I have inherited before it has the chance to surprise me."

"A prudent ambition, Prefect."

"Not ambition. Method." He said it flatly, the correction automatic, the kind of thing he had said to junior officers a hundred times in Germania and meant every time. A soldier who called his discipline ambition had already begun to excuse himself for something. "Bring me the garrison rosters first. Then Gratus's correspondence, whatever order it's in. I'll make my own order of it before the week is out."

Philotas bowed and went, and Pontius returned to the mosaic floor, to a painted vine that some previous hand's chisel had spared, running unbroken along the base of a column into a wall that had once, presumably, held the rest of the garden it depicted. He found he did not mind inheriting a dead king's ambition instead of Rome's. It was, if anything, a cleaner ambition to work with. Herod had wanted to be remembered. Pontius wanted only to be effective, and trusted that the remembering would follow if the effectiveness were thorough enough.

By midmorning he had the rosters. Five cohorts of auxiliary infantry, one wing of cavalry, quartered between Caesarea and the fortress at Jerusalem that overlooked the Temple precinct, a garrison built, as far as he could tell from Gratus's own marginal notes, on the theory that a light hand and long absences would keep the peace better than a firm one. Eleven years of light hands had produced, by Gratus's own account in his final dispatch to Rome, three riots, a burned granary outside Sepphoris, and a standing arrangement by which the high priesthood kept the sacred vestments of the office locked in Roman custody between festivals, released only at Roman convenience, as though piety itself required a permit.

Pontius read that last detail twice and set the dispatch down with something close to satisfaction. It told him more about the province than a year of tax ledgers could have. Gratus had understood, dimly, that the vestments were leverage, and had used them exactly once a year, at feast time, the way a man might spend the one coin in his purse he was certain would buy something. Pontius intended to spend more freely than that. A lever used once a year was not a lever. It was a formality dressed up as strategy.

"He was careful," Pontius said aloud, to no one, tapping the dispatch against the table. "Careful is not the same as strong."

Marcus, who had been arranging scrolls along the far wall since sunrise and had said nothing all morning beyond what his duties required, glanced up at that, then returned to his work without comment. Pontius noted the glance and dismissed it. The boy was Claudia's man, sharp enough in his way, but young, and youth mistook silence for wisdom more often than it earned the right to.

## The Men from Jerusalem

They came four days later, a delegation of six, riding up from Jerusalem on mules with an escort of Temple guards who left their weapons, such as they were, at the edge of the praetorium's outer court, a courtesy Philotas assured him was deliberate and expected to be noticed.

Pontius received them in the audience hall, seated, deliberately, in the chair Gratus had used, a plain curule stool set on a low dais that had once, presumably, held something more ornate before some earlier prefect had judged a king's throne unbecoming to a Roman magistrate's business. He had considered standing to receive them, the way one general might greet another as equals, and decided against it. Equals did not need reminding of the difference between a governor and a delegation. Everyone else did.

Their leader introduced himself as Hanan ben Nahor, an elder of the council, a narrow, gray-bearded man whose Greek was better than Pontius's own and who used it, Pontius suspected within the first quarter hour, as a kind of quiet correction, a way of establishing that Rome's language held no terror for men who had governed themselves, after a fashion, since before Rome existed.

"We come to welcome the new Prefect," Hanan said, "and to lay before him certain matters the previous Prefect left unresolved, so that the new administration may begin its work informed rather than surprised."

"I welcome plain speech," Pontius said. "Speak plainly, then, and we will both save time."

Hanan inclined his head, a gesture that might have been respect or might have been the practiced courtesy of a man who had learned long ago that agreeing to a Roman's terms cost nothing and occasionally purchased something. "The garrison at the Antonia overlooks the Temple courts. It has done so since Herod's day, and the previous Prefect maintained the standing custom that the military standards, which bear the Emperor's image, remain outside Jerusalem's walls, housed instead at Caesarea, and brought into the city only when necessity demanded a show of force the priesthood itself would then be consulted on beforehand."

"A custom," Pontius said. "Not a law."

"A custom is often the only law a conquered people is permitted to keep, Prefect. We ask that you honor it as Gratus did, and as the governor before him."

Pontius let the silence stretch a moment, long enough to be felt, not long enough to be an insult. He had rehearsed some version of this exchange in his own mind a dozen times on the crossing, turning it over the way he turned over a supply problem, reducing it to its essential terms before he let himself feel anything about it.

"Every cohort in this province carries the Emperor's image on its standards," he said. "It is not a provocation. It is the Emperor's army, doing what the Emperor's army does everywhere it is stationed, from Britannia to the Euphrates. I did not come here to invent new grievances. I came to administer what already exists, evenly, the same measure applied to every corner of the province."

"With respect, Prefect, an even measure applied without understanding is not evenness. It is only convenience mistaken for justice." Hanan's voice did not rise. It was, if anything, gentler than before, the gentleness of a man delivering a warning he did not expect to be heeded. "Our law forbids the making of any graven image, and forbids more strictly still that such an image be raised where the eye of the faithful cannot avoid it. The standards are not merely soldiers to us. They are an idol marched past the door of the one house we are commanded to keep clean of idols."

"Then your law and Rome's authority will have to find room to stand in the same street," Pontius said. "I do not intend to remove Roman soldiers from a Roman province because their equipment gives offense. I will keep the standards from the Temple courts themselves, as courtesy, and as acknowledgment of your custom. I will not keep them from the city that Rome governs and Rome garrisons."

He watched the words land, watched the six faces arrange themselves into the same careful blankness Claudia had once described to him, years ago in Rome, as the look of men deciding how much of themselves to show a stranger. He had thought, hearing her say it then, that she meant it as praise for her own perceptiveness. He understood now, watching it happen in his own hall, that she had meant it as a warning, and that he had filed the warning away under the heading of things a wife says to seem wise, rather than the heading of things a governor ought to remember.

Hanan rose, and the others with him, the audience concluding itself by unspoken agreement rather than by any word of Pontius's. "We thank the Prefect for his plain speech," he said. "We will remember it exactly as given."

There was nothing in the sentence Pontius could object to, and everything in it that troubled him faintly as the delegation withdrew, the way a well-aimed blow troubles a man more than a wild one, precisely because it was not wild at all.

## An Even Hand

That evening he found Claudia in the small courtyard at the palace's eastern face, standing over a basin someone had recently drained, the greenish water gone and the stone floor of it still damp, catching the last orange light off the sea.

"They came today," she said, before he could speak. "I heard Philotas's account of it already, secondhand and breathless. He thinks you handled them well."

"And what do you think?"

"I think you told six men who have governed themselves by argument and patience since before Rome had walls that you intend to govern them by measurement instead, and that they heard you perfectly, and agreed to nothing." She said it without heat, watching the empty basin rather than him, and Pontius could not tell, not for the first time, whether she meant it as accusation or simple report.

"I offered them a fair compromise. The standards stay out of the Temple courts."

"You offered them the one thing you were always going to keep anyway, and called it a concession." She looked up at him then. "I don't say you're wrong to hold the line where you've drawn it, Pontius. I say you should understand what it cost you to draw it there, in front of men who will remember the exact words for years, the way your own soldiers remember an order."

He considered arguing the point, and found, standing in the cooling air with the sea working steadily below them, that he did not entirely want to. There was something in her reading of the room that matched, uncomfortably, an unease he had felt himself the moment Hanan had risen to leave, a sense of having won an exchange without having settled anything.

"An even hand," he said instead, half to her and half to the darkening water at their feet, "and an unbending spine. That is the whole of it, Claudia. I did not invent the standards, and I will not apologize for the Emperor's army being what it has always been. If a delegation of elders finds that intolerable, the intolerance is theirs to manage, not mine to remove."

"Perhaps," she said. "Or perhaps a spine only stays unbending until the day it's tested past what it was built to hold, and no man knows which day that is until it has already arrived."

He did not answer that. He watched instead as she crouched at the basin's edge and drew one finger through the last thin film of standing water at its bottom, breaking what little reflection the failing light still offered there. He had no ready answer for that either, and did not try to invent one.

Somewhere beyond the courtyard wall, a trumpet sounded the changing of the watch, brisk and ordinary, the same call he had heard sounded in every garrison town he had ever served in, and Pontius found the sound obscurely comforting, a single fixed point in a country that seemed determined, in every other particular, to refuse him one.
