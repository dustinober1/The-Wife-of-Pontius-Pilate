# Chapter 29: The Recall

## The Order Arrives

Rufus brought her the news before Pontius told her himself, a courtesy she understood, once she had absorbed it, as his own quiet way of giving her a few private moments to compose herself before she had to receive it a second time from her husband's own mouth.

"Vitellius has ordered him to Rome, Domina, to answer to the Emperor for what happened at the mountain. Marcellus arrives within the month to hold the province until Rome decides what comes after." Rufus delivered it standing in the doorway rather than entering fully, a soldier's old instinct for not intruding further into a household's grief than his errand required. "I have served the Prefect ten years, Domina. I do not know how to tell you what it costs me to be the one bringing you this, except to say that I would rather it had been almost any other news I ever carried up these stairs."

She thanked him for the courtesy of coming himself, and sat a long while afterward in the quiet of her own rooms before she went to find Pontius, turning the shape of the sentence over the way she had once turned over a dispatch's meaning from a doorway, ten years and a whole life distant from the young wife who had first heard the word Judaea spoken aloud in a Roman garden and mistaken her own dread for nothing more than the ordinary fear of distance.

She found him in the study, the summons already open before him, and understood from the stillness of his shoulders that he had read it more than once already, the same close, searching attention he had given every document of his early years in office and had, across the three hardest of them, trained himself out of giving to anything at all.

"I am to answer for it in person," he said, not looking up. "Not by dispatch. Not through an intermediary who might soften the account before it reaches Tiberius's own ear. I am to stand before the Emperor himself and explain a mountain full of dead Samaritans who wanted nothing more than an old buried relic."

She crossed the room and stood beside him at the desk, though there was no map to look down at this time, only the summons itself, Vitellius's plain hand naming a mountain full of dead men in language considerably less forgiving than any dispatch Pontius had ever drafted about his own decisions. "What will they ask of you, when you stand before him."

"An accounting, I expect. The same accounting I have owed this province since the morning I gave an order I did not fully weigh before I gave it, except this time the accounting will be given to a man old enough and tired enough of governors' excuses that I do not expect my own explanations to carry the weight they once carried with Sejanus." He set the summons down at last, and she saw in the gesture none of the deliberate economy he had once brought to every closed matter, only a plain and unguarded tiredness. "I have spent ten years believing a governor answered, in the end, only to Rome's ledgers. I understand now that a man answers, eventually, to whatever he has actually done, regardless of how carefully the ledger says he has accounted for it."

"When do we leave."

"As soon as Marcellus arrives to take the province from me properly. A matter of weeks, Rufus tells me, though I confess I have stopped trusting my own sense of how quickly weeks pass, these past several days." He looked up at her then, and she saw in his face an exhaustion that went beyond the office, beyond even the mountain itself, an exhaustion she recognized, watching it settle into the lines she had watched deepen across ten years of this marriage, as the particular tiredness of a man finally made to stop and look directly at a reckoning he had spent three years learning how to outrun.

## What Ten Years Leaves Behind

The packing, when it began in earnest, bore almost no resemblance to the hurried three days she remembered from Rome a decade before, agents summoned, a domus shuttered room by room, farewells exchanged with a haste that had left no room for reflection. This packing unfolded slowly, over weeks rather than days, the household disassembling itself with the particular grief of people closing a chapter they had not chosen to close, tax ledgers and correspondence and ten years of a marriage's accumulated small belongings sorted into what would travel and what would be left behind for whatever governor came after Marcellus to inherit.

She thought, more than once during those weeks, of Sulpicia, dead now these four years past of a fever the household physician had never fully named, buried in the small Roman cemetery outside Caesarea's walls rather than carried home to the soil she had always sworn she meant to be buried in, and understood that her old nurse's grave was one more thing this recall would ask her to leave behind unfinished, a grief she had carried quietly through the silent years and would now carry onto a ship without ever having properly closed the account of it.

Marcus managed the inventory this time with none of the boyish uncertainty she remembered from Ostia, a man of twenty-six directing porters and household staff with the same careful competence he brought to everything, though she caught him once, standing over the same cedar trunk that had once held her record scrolls on the crossing out, running his thumb along its brass corner with an expression she recognized as its own private grief for a decade he was not yet ready to have end.

## Caiaphas, Also Removed

It was Philotas who brought her the strange, final irony of it, delivered with the same careful economy he had always given the household's news. "Vitellius has deposed Caiaphas as well, Domina, in the same season he recalled the Prefect, though for reasons of his own that have nothing directly to do with the mountain. The high priest who outlasted every governor before this one will not outlast this one after all."

She turned the fact over a long while, thinking of Caiaphas's calm audience in the hall that winter after Sejanus fell, the unhurried economy of a man who no longer needed to press an advantage because the advantage pressed itself, and understood that the same current which had finally caught her husband had reached, in the same season, the one man in this province who had always seemed, across ten years, to stand safely above every current that troubled anyone beneath him. She did not know whether to feel the symmetry as justice or only as further proof that nothing in this province, however carefully built, had ever been as permanent as the men who built it believed.

## Farewell to Tamar

Tamar came to her on the last evening before the ship was to sail, and they sat together in the small courtyard the way they had sat together after Rivkah's death ten years before, saying little at first, the silence between them doing most of the speaking either of them still trusted herself to manage.

"I will not see you again, Domina, I think." Tamar said it plainly, without the careful reserve she sometimes still wore for harder truths. "You are going home, to whatever home means to you now after ten years away from it, and I am staying in the only home I have ever known, and I do not think either of our lives affords us the journey back to find the other again."

"I do not know that Rome is home to me any longer, Tamar. I am not certain it ever was, in the way you mean it." Claudia took her hand, the first time in ten years of friendship she had done so without some crisis prompting the gesture. "You gave me more of whatever I am becoming than any single person in that city ever gave me, including the ones raised me from a child. I do not know how to thank a woman for that in a single evening in a courtyard."

"Then do not thank me, Domina. Only carry it the way you have carried everything else these ten years, quietly, and put it to use wherever you find yourself needing it." Tamar pressed her hand once, and released it. "I told you once that a hope worth keeping survives the day it is tested without breaking itself. You have been tested a great many days since I first said that to you. I do not think, watching you now, that the hope broke."

## Simon's Question

Simon found her the same evening, lingering near the kitchen stores with a hesitance she had come to recognize, across ten years, as the particular shape of a question he had not yet decided he had the right to ask her.

"Will you take me with you, Domina, or am I to stay." He said it plainly, once she had given him the small nod that told him she had noticed him waiting. "I do not know which answer I want you to give me. I have a home here, whatever this household's fortunes, kin who walked to the river with me and stayed to hear the teacher afterward. I do not know what Rome would want with a cook's boy grown into a man who has watched what I have watched in this province."

"I do not know, myself, what waits for any of us in Rome, Simon, and I would not ask you to cross a sea for a household that cannot promise you anything better on the far side of it than the life you already have here." She considered him a long moment, the boy she remembered from the Jordan grown now into a steadiness she had watched settle into him slowly, the way it had settled into Marcus in a different register. "Stay, if staying is what your own conscience tells you. I release you from anything this household ever asked of you, and I thank you for everything you gave it that was never, strictly, part of your duties."

He thanked her, visibly relieved to have been given the choice rather than only the instruction, and she watched him go with a small ache she had not expected, one more thread of this decade's life she would carry away unfinished, the way she carried Sulpicia's grave and Tamar's courtyard and a hundred smaller griefs this recall was asking her to leave behind without the mercy of a proper closing.

## The Ship at Caesarea

The harbor at Caesarea offered none of Ostia's gray, salt-damp chill, the eastern morning already warm and bright over the mole Pontius had once had built to his own exacting specifications in the first year of an office he was now leaving under a cloud considerably darker than the one he had arrived beneath.

Marcus oversaw the loading of the last chests with the same brisk competence he brought to everything, and Claudia stood apart from the activity a while, watching the household's remaining possessions carried up the gangway, ten years of a life reduced to what a single ship's hold could carry, and found herself thinking, without quite meaning to, of the brass key she still kept from a Roman garden gate long since shuttered to some agent's keeping, wondering whether the house it opened even still stood, and whether the key would open anything at all by the time she next had cause to try it.

Pontius came to stand beside her at the rail once the ship had cleared the harbor mouth, Judaea's coastline already beginning to thin behind them into the same flat gray line Italy had once thinned into on a different crossing a decade before, and neither of them spoke for a long while, the two of them keeping the same silent watch they had kept over an emptying tribunal three years past, over a darkening sky the same afternoon, over every hard morning this marriage had asked them to stand through together rather than alone.

"I do not know what waits for us in Rome," he said finally, and she heard in his voice none of the certainty that had once carried him up a different gangway with a wooden eagle already claiming a city he had not yet set foot in. "I do not know whether Tiberius will hear my account and set it aside, or whether this crossing ends somewhere considerably worse than a hard audience with an old man in a villa on Capri."

"Then we will learn it together, whatever it turns out to be." She said it and heard, saying it, the same words she had once given him a decade before, on a different deck, before either of them had learned what this life would actually cost to live. "We have learned everything else that way. I do not see why this should be different."

He did not answer that, only reached for her hand at the rail, and Claudia let him hold it, watching the coast of the only home she had known for ten years narrow by degrees into a thin dark line and then into nothing at all, the wind off the open sea already colder than anything Caesarea's harbor had prepared her for, carrying them west toward whatever the crossing still meant to make of either of them before it finally set them down.
