# Chapter 27: Three Years of Silence

## What Three Years Wore Away

Three years had passed since the Passover that neither of them spoke of directly anymore, and Claudia had come to measure the passing less by any single change than by the accumulation of small silences the household had learned to keep around certain subjects the way a body learned, over time, to favor an old wound without any longer remembering the day it was first taken.

Caesarea had not changed. The aqueduct still carried its water from the springs at Bethlehem exactly as the surveys had promised it would, the Antonia's replacement garrison still drilled in its ordinary rotations, the market still sold its fish honestly weighed or dishonestly by the same stalls that had always weighed it one way or the other. It was Pontius who had changed, by degrees too gradual for her to mark any single day as the day the change had properly begun, so that she found herself now, in this tenth year of his governorship, married to a man whose company she recognized in outline but no longer entirely in substance, the way a familiar coastline still read as home from a ship's deck even after the ship itself had carried you somewhere the coastline's old maps no longer quite described.

## What Pontius Had Become

He governed harder now than she had ever watched him govern, a hardness she understood, watching him, as the final and most thorough version of the fear she had first heard him admit to plainly the night Sejanus fell. A dispute that would once have earned a petitioner a full hearing now earned him a ruling delivered in half the time, decisive rather than deliberated, and she had learned to recognize the decisiveness as something closer to a man closing doors before they could be used against him than a man confident in the rightness of what lay behind them.

"You ruled against the wool merchants again this morning," she said to him one evening, the same complaint she had raised once before, years earlier, over a different ruling that had gone the other way. "You did not hear their full argument."

"I heard enough of it to know where it was going, and a governor who spends an hour on every dispute that wants only ten minutes teaches the whole province that delay is itself a kind of leverage against him." He did not look up from the dispatch he was reading, and she noted, watching him, that his hand no longer went still over a document the way it once had when a hard truth in it had cost him something to read. He read everything now with the same unbroken attention, hard news and ordinary news alike, as though he had trained himself, across three years, no longer to let any single fact land differently from any other. "I do not have the years left in this office to spend them the way I once spent them, weighing every petitioner's grievance as though the province had nothing better to ask of me than patience."

She heard, beneath the administrative certainty, the same fear that had governed every decision of the past three years, the fear of a man who had watched his patron fall and his own protection dissolve overnight, and who had concluded, she understood now with a clarity she had not possessed at the time, that the single tribunal morning she still could not speak of directly had cost him something even that fear had never fully priced before it. He did not speak of that morning. He had not spoken of it, in any direct way, since the night he told her he did not know what he believed the dream had meant. She had learned, across three years, not to ask him to.

## What Claudia Had Become

She had changed too, though the changing showed itself less in anything Pontius would have named a difference than in a private practice she now kept as faithfully as she had once kept the burning of her own pages. She no longer burned them. The chest beside her bed held, by now, four such pages rolled tight beside the brass garden key and the girlhood ribbon, each one written in the aftermath of some report that had reached her and refused to settle into an answer, each one a small private admission that she had stopped requiring herself to resolve before she was willing to let it exist.

She spent more of her days now with Tamar than the household's ordinary business strictly required, and had begun, without quite naming the practice to herself as anything more than an old friendship deepening, to use whatever small authority a governor's wife carried in this province on behalf of people who would never otherwise have had occasion to reach her. A widow whose son had been conscripted for labor on a public work she quietly arranged reassigned to lighter duty. A family facing a tax assessment calculated, as the wool merchants' had once been, on a season crueler than the one it claimed to measure, whose appeal she carried to Philotas herself rather than let it die unheard in the ordinary channels. None of it was large. None of it undid anything her husband's harder governing had done or would go on doing. She had come to understand, across these three years, that the smallness of it was not a failure of the gesture but its entire point, the same lesson Tamar had once given her at a mourning she had barely understood at the time, that what is broken is not broken forever, and that the mending available to any one person was rarely the size of the breaking it answered.

"You have become someone your husband does not quite recognize, Domina, I think," Tamar said to her once, watching her seal a private letter to Philotas on the tax matter. "I do not say that as a criticism."

"I know you do not." Claudia set the seal down, considering the observation with the same care she had learned to give any of Tamar's quieter remarks. "I am not certain I entirely recognize myself either, some days. I only know that I would rather be someone who does these small useless things than someone who has stopped noticing there is anything left worth doing them for."

## Marcus, Grown Quieter

Marcus had grown into these same three years in his own way, and Claudia found, watching him move through the household's daily business with the same careful competence he had always brought to it, that she could no longer entirely separate the young man he had become from the boy who had once run a scroll through a capital tribunal at the risk of the lash. He was near thirty now, though the household still called him boy from habit more than accuracy, and he had begun, in the past year, to keep his own small store of learning apart from his duties, a handful of scrolls he read by lamplight after the house had settled, philosophy and history mostly, purchased with wages he might otherwise have spent on anything a young man in his position was more ordinarily expected to want.

"You are teaching yourself something, Marcus," she said to him once, finding him at his reading later than the household's business required of him. "What is it you mean to do with it."

"I do not know yet, Domina. I only know that a man who has watched what I have watched these past three years finds he wants to understand more of the world than his duties alone require him to understand, whether or not the understanding ever changes what he is asked to do within it." He set the scroll aside, his old careful discretion still governing exactly how much he would say even to her. "I think, perhaps, I am only doing what you yourself have been doing, Domina, in your own way, these past three years. Keeping something for myself that the household's ordinary business does not ask of me, because I have found I no longer trust myself to be only what my duties say I am."

She had no better answer for that than she had ever had for any of Marcus's quiet observations, and let the subject drop, understanding that whatever Marcus was building for himself in these private hours was his own account to keep, the way her own chest of unburned pages was hers.

## A Marriage at Distance

They kept, by now, separate hours more often than shared ones, Pontius given over almost entirely to the province's harder business, Claudia to the household's quieter one, and she found, some evenings, that she missed the sharper years of their marriage more than she would have predicted missing them, the standards crisis, the aqueduct's aftermath, even the raw grief of the colonnade riot, because those years had at least still asked something direct of both of them together, a shared crisis rather than a shared silence.

"Do you ever think of that morning," she asked him once, late enough in an evening that the wine had loosened something in each of them that the daylight hours kept carefully guarded. "The one neither of us names."

"Every day I govern this province." He said it without looking at her, his eyes on the lamp rather than on her face. "I do not think of it the way you think of it, Claudia. I do not carry a dream, or a tomb, or whatever your household still whispers about among itself when it thinks I am not listening. I carry the accounting of it. What it cost me, what it did not cost me, what I would do differently if a council ever brought me a morning like it again, though I pray to whatever god still troubles to listen that one never does." He turned to her then, and she saw, in the lamplight, an exhaustion she had not let herself fully see in him before, three years compressed into the lines at the corners of his eyes. "I envy you, some nights, whatever it is you carry instead. I do not think mine has ever once let me sleep as easily as I imagine yours does."

She did not tell him that hers did not let her sleep easily either, only differently, and let the silence between them settle back into its accustomed shape, the two of them sitting together in a single room and carrying, each alone, the same morning neither had found a way to set down.

## What Still Waited

She had begun to notice, in the past several months, a new note in the dispatches that reached Pontius from beyond Judaea's own borders, reports of a Samaritan matter building somewhere in the hill country around Gerizim, a prophet of their own gathering a following with promises to uncover something sacred buried since Moses' own time, a following Pontius read, when he read it at all, with the same flat administrative attention he now gave everything, filing it alongside a hundred other provincial disturbances not yet large enough to trouble him personally.

"The Samaritans have their own mountain and their own prophets, the same as this province has always had its own," he said to her once, dismissing the matter with the same certainty he now dismissed most things, "and a mountain crowd is easier to disperse than a city crowd, should it ever come to that, which I do not expect it will. They gather to dig for old vessels a dead lawgiver supposedly buried, Claudia, not to crown a king. It is a fool's errand, not a rebellion, and I have learned, across ten years in this province, to save my worry for the crowds that gather around a claim rather than a legend."

She had heard him weigh a crowd's danger by exactly that measure once before, along a different lakeshore, in a chapter of this marriage she no longer let herself revisit without cost, and found she could not share his confidence that a fool's errand and a rebellion were as easily distinguished, in practice, as his ledger's tidy categories made them sound.

She did not yet know to be afraid of it specifically, only that she had learned, across three years of watching him govern by an ever-narrowing margin of patience, that the next crisis to reach this desk would not be met by the man who had once, six years earlier, backed down from a stadium of bared throats rather than order a massacre. It would be met by the man sitting across from her now, hardened past the point of measuring a crisis by anything except how quickly it could be closed, and she found, watching him read the Samaritan dispatch a final time before setting it aside for the evening, that she could not entirely say which of them she feared more for: the province that would eventually meet that hardened version of him in earnest, or the man himself, who no longer seemed to notice how far the hardening had carried him from whoever he had been the day she first pressed a compass into his hand and told him he had not yet mapped their pride.
