# Chapter 24: What the City Heard

## The Household Waits

Claudia did not go down into the city that day, and did not ask Pontius whether she might, understanding without needing to be told that whatever remained of the morning's business belonged now to the soldiers and the crowd and not to a governor's wife who had already spent the whole of her welcome on a single note delivered at a knife's edge. She kept to the palace's inner rooms through the hours that followed, finding she could not settle to any ordinary task, the household accounts abandoned half-tallied on the table before her, a length of wool left untouched on the loom where she had set it down that morning without any memory, now, of having done so.

Pontius withdrew to his own correspondence not long after their exchange on the terrace, and she understood, watching him go, that he had chosen paperwork the way he had once chosen a map, a fixed and countable thing to hold between himself and an hour he did not yet know how to carry any other way. She did not follow him. She sat instead at her writing table with a blank page before her that she did not fill, the reed pen resting untouched at its edge, and found she had nothing to burn this time, because she had nothing yet that had cooled enough into words to need burning.

The household moved around her through the late morning with the hushed, careful economy it always kept on the days its mistress's grief showed clearly enough that even the newer servants had learned to walk more softly past her door. She heard, at some distance, the ordinary machinery of a feast day continuing without her, water drawn, a loom's shuttle somewhere below, voices pitched low enough that she could not make out their words, and found the ordinariness of it almost harder to bear than any single loud report would have been, the world continuing to turn its small daily gears around an hour that felt, to her, as though it ought to have stopped everything in the province from moving at all.

## The Sky Changes

It was Tamar who first remarked on the light, sometime past the sixth hour, coming to find Claudia in the small room off the peristyle with an unease in her bearing that Claudia had not seen in her since Rivkah's death.

"Look at the sky, Domina." Tamar had already crossed to the window before Claudia rose to join her. Overhead, the sun's ordinary brightness had thinned into something the color of old bronze, a darkness gathering at the day's height that belonged to no season Claudia had lived through in six years of Judaean summers, no storm cloud, no dust wind off the desert, only a slow dimming that made the whole city below seem to hold its breath along with the sky. "The old women in the market are already saying it is a sign. I do not know what it is a sign of, Domina, or whether it is only the season doing something none of us have troubled to notice before in six years of standing in this same courtyard at this same hour."

They stood together at the window a long while, the light continuing to fail by degrees too gradual to mark precisely, until the courtyard beneath them had taken on the flat, drained cast of a day remembered rather than a day being lived. Claudia thought, without wanting to, of the basin still standing at the tribunal's edge that morning, of black water at Ostia, of a fountain in a Roman garden years before any of this had begun, as though the whole of her life's water and now the sky itself had conspired to answer a question she had never quite found the courage to ask aloud.

## Marcus at the Edge

Marcus went down into the city in the early afternoon, on an errand he did not fully explain to her and she did not press him to account for, and returned some two hours later with the stillness of a man who had seen something he had not yet found a way to set down into an ordinary report.

"I went as far as the hill outside the wall, Domina, where the soldiers keep the crowd back from the crosses themselves." He said it plainly, without the careful hedging he usually brought to news he judged might unsettle her, as though the day itself had worn past the point where softening anything further served any purpose. "I did not go closer than the soldiers allowed. I would not have gone closer had they allowed it."

"What did you see."

"Three men, Domina, the teacher among them, though I could not tell from that distance which of the other two had done what to earn their own crosses. A crowd larger than I expected, though quieter than I expected too, some of them weeping, some jeering, most simply watching in the particular silence a crowd keeps when it has come to see something it does not yet know how to feel about." Marcus's jaw did its old tightening thing, and this time he did not try to master it before he continued. "There was a woman near the front, Domina, who I am told is his mother, though I could not have picked her from the crowd myself without being told. And a handful of others who stayed close through the whole of it, when most who had followed him through the city these past days had already scattered, I am told, sometime in the night."

"Did he say anything. At the end."

"Something, Domina, though I was too far to hear it clearly, and the crowd's own noise took most of it before it could carry as far as where I stood. I am told, by those closer than I was, that some of what he said sounded less like a man protesting his own death than a man settling some final account he had been keeping his whole life, though I would not trust every report of it equally, given how quickly a crowd's memory of a thing tends to improve itself once the thing is over."

"You said soldiers kept the crowd back. Was there any disturbance."

"None that I saw, Domina, which surprised me more than anything else about the hill. A crowd that size, watching a man some of them had called a king only five days past, and no disturbance at all, only a great deal of quiet weeping and a few who left before it was finished, unable, I think, to watch the whole of it through." Marcus glanced toward the window, where the strange bronze light Tamar had first remarked on was only now beginning to thin back toward an ordinary evening. "I stayed until the sky changed, Domina. I do not think I could have told you, standing there, why I felt I needed to stay for that part particularly. Only that it seemed the kind of thing a person ought to witness firsthand, not only hear reported afterward, whatever it turns out to have meant."

She let that sit between them a moment, watching Marcus's hands, the ink-stain still faint on his thumb after all these years, and thought of how carefully he had always carried whatever the household needed carried, the note through the crowd that morning, the accounts he kept honest when no one troubled to check them, this report now, delivered as gently as its content allowed. "Thank you for going, Marcus. I did not ask it of you."

"I know you did not, Domina. I went because I did not think any of us in this house should be able to say, afterward, that we let it happen forty yards away and never once troubled to look at what our own household's warning had failed to stop."

## What the Household Carried

Simon kept to the kitchen through the whole of that afternoon, and Claudia found him there near evening scrubbing a pot that did not, by any measure she could see, still need scrubbing, his shoulders bent in the same unresolved posture she remembered from the week John died.

"I keep thinking, Domina, of what he told me. About the mending already begun." He did not look up from the pot as he said it. "I don't know how a man who mends the world ends up on a cross. Same as any thief the Prefect's soldiers ever hung there." He shook his head. "I don't have the learning for it. Those two things won't sit together in me."

"Neither do I, Simon." She said it honestly, finding she had no comfort readier than the truth of her own confusion to offer him. "I do not think either of us is meant to have it settled today. Perhaps some things are only carried a long while before a person finds out what they were for."

He nodded slowly at that, the way he had nodded at her once before over a similar uncertainty, and returned to the pot, and she left him to it, understanding that his grief, like her own unnamed disquiet, wanted time, not an answer neither of them yet possessed.

Tamar found her again as she crossed back through the peristyle, and walked with her a while in a silence Claudia had come, across seven years, to recognize as its own kind of speech between them. "I have been thinking, Domina, of what I told you once, sitting with my aunt's mourning bread between us. That what is broken is not broken forever." She paused at the edge of the colonnade, looking out toward the darkening slope beyond the walls where the crosses still stood. "I do not know how to hold that next to what I watched the sky do today. Or what Marcus watched on that hill." She was quiet a moment. "The day was not easy to carry, Domina. I carried it anyway. Perhaps that is what the hope was always going to ask of us."

Claudia had no answer ready for that either, only the same honest confusion she had offered Simon. Walking on beside Tamar in the day's failing light, she no longer minded quite so much having no answer, as though some part of her had begun, without her fully choosing it, to trust the carrying itself more than she trusted any settled account of what the carrying was for.

## Nightfall

The sky had cleared by the time full dark came on, the ordinary stars returning to their ordinary places as though the afternoon's strange bronze light had never troubled them at all, and Claudia stood once more at the terrace balustrade, looking down at a Lithostrotos empty now of every trace of the morning's crowd, swept clean by servants before the evening meal, the basin long since carried away.

Pontius found her there, his correspondence finally set aside for the night, and stood beside her without speaking for a long while, the two of them watching the dark city settle into whatever quiet a feast night still afforded it.

"Rufus tells me the body has already been asked for," he said at last. "A council member, one who did not vote for the sentence this morning, by Rufus's account, has petitioned to take it down before the Sabbath begins, and to bury it in a tomb of his own rather than let it go to the common ground the law would otherwise send it to."

"Will you grant it."

"I have already granted it. It costs me nothing to grant a dead man's body to a councillor's private grief, and it costs me, perhaps, some small measure of the province's good faith to refuse a courtesy any other prisoner's family might reasonably have asked of me." He looked out over the emptied pavement, and Claudia heard in his voice the same flatness he had used to give every order that day, though something beneath the flatness now sounded less like administration and more like a man testing, privately, how much weight a single ordinary decision could still bear after a morning that had asked so much more of him than any decision ever had before. "I do not think, Claudia, that granting it undoes anything. I think it is simply the last thing I have left to give, now that everything else about today is already decided and cannot be called back."

"Rufus sent word of the sky as well, I imagine. Whatever it was."

"He did, though he had less to say about it than I expected of a man who has never once, in my hearing, admitted to being unsettled by anything short of a legion's own mutiny." Pontius's mouth showed the ghost of something that was not quite a smile. "He told me only that the garrison found it easier to keep discipline through the dark than through the noise beforehand, and that he did not intend to write it into any official report, since a Roman dispatch has no line for a sky a soldier cannot explain and does not wish to be asked to."

"And you. What will you write."

"The same flat account I have written of every hard thing this office has asked me to survive, I expect. A disturbance, a sentence, a peace restored." He said it without any of the pride such an account might once have carried for him, in the earlier years of this marriage, when a clean report had still felt to him like its own kind of victory. "I do not know how a man writes the truer account, Claudia, even if Rome would trouble to read it. I am not certain I know the truer account myself yet, tonight, though I suspect I will spend a great deal longer than tonight learning what it actually was."

She said nothing to that, only stood beside him as she had stood beside him that morning, the two of them keeping their long, silent watch over a city that would, by tomorrow, have already begun forgetting how strange its own sky had looked that afternoon.
