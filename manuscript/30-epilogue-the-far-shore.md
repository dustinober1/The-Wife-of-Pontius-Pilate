# Epilogue: The Far Shore

## Word at Misenum

The ship put in at Misenum for water and news before making the final short run north to Ostia, and it was there, on a quay considerably smaller and quieter than the one they had once departed from a decade before, that word reached them of what had happened on the mountain across the bay while they were still three days out at sea.

Tiberius was dead. He had died at his own villa, an old man's death, unremarkable in every particular except its timing, and Pontius stood at the rail reading the dockmaster's plain account of it with an expression Claudia had not seen on him since the morning a different summons had first reached him at a different harbor, ten years and an entire life before this one.

"He died before I could stand before him." Pontius said it slowly, as though testing whether the sentence changed its meaning the more times he turned it over. "There is no one left in Rome now who was waiting to hear my account of the mountain. Caligula rules in his place, and I do not know, standing here, whether a new Emperor inherits his predecessor's unfinished summons or lets it die quietly with the old man who issued it."

"What will you do."

"I do not know yet. I do not think anyone in Rome knows yet either, which may be its own kind of mercy, or its own kind of sentence deferred rather than lifted." He set the dockmaster's account down at last, and looked out at the bay, Capri's dark shape visible at the edge of it, the island where an old man had spent a decade governing an empire by letter rather than by presence, dying at last in the same remove he had always kept from everything he ruled. "I have spent ten years believing every question I could not yet answer would eventually be answered for me, one way or another, by a ledger, a report, a summons. I am beginning to understand that some questions simply go on unanswered, Claudia, and that a man must learn to live inside that unanswering rather than waiting it out."

She had no comfort readier than the truth she had carried these ten years herself, and offered him that instead. "I have been learning the same thing a good deal longer than you have, I think. It is survivable. I cannot promise you more than that. I am not certain more than that has ever been promised to anyone."

He looked at her a long moment, and she saw in his face the same plain, unguarded honesty she remembered from the story he had once told her of his own first meeting with a woman he barely knew, admitting to nothing more than a fear of Rome forgetting him, before either of them had understood what the admitting would eventually cost either one of them to carry. "You have changed a great deal these ten years, Claudia. I do not think I have watched it happen so much as woken, every so often, to discover it had already happened without my noticing."

"You have changed too. Not always for the better, and I do not think either of us needs me to pretend otherwise tonight." She said it gently, watching Capri's dark shape hold steady at the edge of the bay. "But you are standing here telling me you no longer expect every question to be answered for you. I do not think the man I married in that Roman garden, certain of a red seal and a praetorship before he turned forty, would have known how to say that sentence."

"No," he said. "I do not think he would have."

## The Chest Opened

She went below that evening while Pontius remained on deck with the ship's captain, working out whatever practical questions a governor recalled under an unresolved cloud still had to settle before the final crossing to Ostia, and opened, for the first time since Judaea's coast had thinned behind them, the small chest she had carried up the gangway at Caesarea alongside everything else this decade had left her worth keeping.

The brass key lay where it had always lain, cold and unremarkable, a garden gate in Rome she no longer knew whether still stood or whether some agent's careless keeping had let it fall to ruin these ten years past. The girlhood ribbon beside it. And the five pages, rolled tight, that she had written and refused to burn across the years since a colonnade first taught her what her own silence cost, unrolled now, one by one, in the lamp's low light, her own hand looking younger to her on the earliest of them than she remembered feeling at the time she had written it.

She read them through slowly, *I do not believe in omens* crossed out on its own page by the very fact of the page's continued existence, *I watched a basin of water fail to answer a question* sitting beside it in a hand grown steadier by the time she had written it. Reading her own decade back to herself in the small swaying lamplight of a ship three days from Ostia, she was not the same woman who had once believed a warning worth burning the moment it cooled. She did not know, closing the chest again, whether what she had become had a single true name, faith or conscience or only a grief that had finally worn itself into something usable. For the first time in all these years of turning the question over, she no longer needed the naming settled before she trusted the thing itself.

She thought, folding the pages away, of her guardian's flat voice asking a nine-year-old girl whether she understood what it meant to be useful to his house, and of the answer she had given him then, that it meant doing what was asked and asking nothing in return. A decade and a whole marriage removed from that tablinum, she could see now that she had spent the better part of her life answering a question no one had actually asked her a second time, and that the five pages in her lap were the closest she had ever come to answering it differently.

## What Marcus Kept

Marcus found her on deck again as the last light was going, the same ink-stain still faint on his thumb after all these years, and she looked at it a moment longer than she had looked at it in a long while, remembering a tablinum in Rome, a guardian's cold instruction that she was loved for being useful to his house, a lesson she had carried unexamined for the better part of her life before this decade had finally taught her to set it down.

"You will be freed, when we reach Rome, if you want it," she told him, the decision arriving in her only as she spoke it, though some part of her, saying it, recognized it had been building toward the offer for longer than she had let herself notice. "Whatever I once believed I owed this household for the ink on your hands and mine, I do not believe it any longer. You have given this family ten years of a usefulness no one ever adequately named or paid for. I would rather send you into whatever comes next as a man who chose to stay near us, than as one who never had the choosing."

Marcus said nothing for a long moment, his jaw doing the old tightening thing. She could see him working it out, in real time, an answer he had not expected to be asked for. "I do not know yet what I would do with a freedom I have never once had cause to imagine for myself, Domina. I think, perhaps, I would still choose to stay near you a while longer, freedom or no. I have watched this family carry more than most families are asked to carry. I find I am not finished watching yet."

## The Far Shore

The final crossing to Ostia came at dawn two days later, the same gray, salt-damp light she remembered from the morning they had first left it, a decade and an entire other life ago. The water beneath the ship's hull ran differently to her eye now than it had run then. The sea itself had not changed. She had simply stopped needing it to answer her.

She stood at the rail with Pontius beside her, his fate still unresolved, an old Emperor's death having neither condemned nor acquitted him, only left the question standing open the way so many questions of this decade had been left standing open, unanswered and, she was beginning to understand, survivable regardless. The coast of Italy thickened ahead of them out of the morning mist, the same flat gray line that had once dissolved behind her into nothing, now gathering itself back into something she would have to learn, all over again, how to call home.

She pressed her thumb against the seam of her palm, the old childhood habit, and felt beneath it, faint and permanent, the same small crescent of ink that had never fully washed from under her nail in thirty years, and thought, watching the shore arrive rather than recede for the first time in this marriage, that she did not know what waited for either of them on it. She knew only that she meant to walk into it with her eyes open, the way she had not always managed across the ten years now closing behind her, and that whatever she was, faith or conscience or only a woman who had finally stopped needing the water to give her back a whole reflection, she meant to carry it ashore with her rather than leave it, unclaimed, somewhere out on the crossing.
