# Chapter 3: Landfall

## The Shore of Judaea

Land came before the coastline did, a smell first, dry and resinous, carried out on a wind that had lost the sea's clean bite somewhere over the last few miles of water.

Claudia stood at the rail where she had stood for three weeks, through Messana and Crete and the long gray stretch of open water after, and watched the smudge on the horizon resolve itself by degrees into something with edges. White stone first, catching the morning sun in a way that made her think, absurdly, of teeth. Then the harbor itself, built where no harbor had any right to be: a great curved arm of masonry reaching out into water that had no natural shelter to offer it, breakwaters sunk deliberately into the sea's own resistance.

"Herod built that," Pontius said, coming to stand beside her, close enough that his shoulder brushed hers. He had shaved that morning, the first time in four days. The effort of it, the small vanity of arriving clean-faced into his own province, told her more about his nerves than anything he might have said aloud. "Sunk the foundations in volcanic ash brought from across the sea. Gratus's dispatches said the engineers thought he was mad. Building a harbor where the coast refused to give him one."

"And did it hold?"

"It held." He said it with the grudging respect of one engineer for another's audacity, and no curiosity at all about the men who had died quarrying the stone. "The man understood that a king who builds is a king who is remembered. Fifty years dead, and half of what we govern by is still his."

Below them, the water changed color as the ship came into the harbor's shelter, the churned gray-green of the open sea giving way to something calmer and clearer, close enough to the surface that Claudia could see her own reflection break and reassemble against the hull's slow drift. She had let the ocean's blank indifference carry her past the memory of the fountain in Rome for days. Now, in these first few feet of harbor-still water, her face came back to her in pieces, the way it had that last evening in the garden, and she found she did not welcome the return of it.

Marcus appeared at her elbow with the quiet efficiency that had made him indispensable somewhere over the Aegean, a wax tablet already in hand, his knuckles white around the stylus from three weeks of counting cargo he could not risk losing.

"The harbor master's boat is putting out to meet us, Domina. Twelve oars, by the look of it. They mean to make an impression." He did not look up from the tablet. "I've confirmed the household goods against the manifest twice more since Crete. Nothing lost but the second amphora of the Falernian, and that to the crew's thirst rather than the sea's."

"You've done well, Marcus." She meant it, and something in her tone made him glance up, briefly, before returning to his count. He had been barely more than a boy when they left Rome, promoted out of some steward's errand-running into the harder work of managing a household across an ocean, and she had watched the crossing age him in ways he seemed not to have noticed in himself yet.

The city came up out of the water slowly, the way distant things always resisted being known all at once. A theater cut into a natural rise of ground, its stone tiers pale as bone. An amphitheater beyond it, longer than anything Rome itself could boast outside the old Circus. Broad paved streets ran in the ruthless straight lines Romans loved, cutting through what must once have been fishing village and scrubland alike. Temples with columns gleamed white in a way that made Claudia's eyes ache after weeks of grey water.

It looked, she thought, like Rome had been poured out onto a foreign coast and left to harden before anyone had asked whether the ground beneath it wanted the shape.

"It's beautiful," she said, because it was, and because she did not yet have words for the other thing she felt, watching Roman marble insist itself onto land that had clearly belonged, until very recently and by force, to somebody else entirely.

"It's Rome," Pontius said, with the particular satisfaction of a man recognizing his own reflection in unfamiliar water. He did not seem to hear the difference between the two things she had almost said.

Behind the white theater and the ruled streets, though, the land itself refused to be entirely erased. Beyond the last row of colonnades she could see hills the color of old bread, dry and folded, running back from the coast toward a horizon that owed nothing to any architect. Goats picked along a slope where no road had been laid. A line of women moved along a footpath worn into the hillside by nothing but the accumulated weight of feet, generations of them, long before the harbor's first stone had been sunk. Rome had built its city facing the sea, she noticed, facing home, facing away from the interior, as though the coastline were the only Judaea that mattered and the country behind it merely the price of holding the coastline. She wondered, watching the goats and the worn path and the hills that curved away into country no engineer's ledger had troubled to describe, how long a government could go on governing a shoreline while pretending the country behind it did not exist.

## The Palace by the Sea

The praetorium had been Herod's palace before it was anything of Pontius's, and it wore its second use the way an old soldier wears a young man's armor: serviceably, with the seams showing.

They came to it in the late afternoon, through streets that emptied and refilled around their small procession with a curiosity Claudia could feel more than see. Dark eyes tracked the litter from doorways and market stalls, then slid away the moment she turned to meet them. The palace itself sat at the edge of the promontory, close enough to the sea that she could hear the surf beneath the noise of the household staff assembling to receive them. The first thing she noticed, stepping down from the litter, was the smell of salt cutting through incense somebody had burned too generously in the entrance hall, as though perfume alone could announce that Rome had arrived to stay.

"The audience hall," said the household's chief freedman, a thin, anxious Greek named Philotas who had served three prefects before Pontius and clearly hoped to outlast a fourth, gesturing them through a columned entry into a space built for a king's vanity, not a governor's business. Herod's mosaics still covered the floor: vines and pomegranates worked in green and gold tesserae, a king's private appetite for beauty surviving fifty years past his death and one violent change of ownership. Along the walls, someone had already begun the work of scraping away the more ambiguous figures, the ones too close to graven images for Roman taste to leave untouched, leaving pale ghost-shapes in the plaster where painted birds or beasts had been.

Pontius walked the length of the hall slowly, running a hand along a marble pilaster, testing the building the way she had once watched him test a horse before purchase, looking for the give in it, the places where age or neglect had made it less than it claimed to be.

"He built for permanence," Pontius said, half to her, half to the room. "You can feel it in the walls. A man who never expected to lose anything."

"He lost his own sons," Claudia said. "If Marcus's letters from your uncle's household are to be believed. Executed on his own order, near the end."

Pontius's hand stilled on the pilaster. "Every king fears his own succession. It does not follow that he feared losing the kingdom."

"No," she agreed. "Perhaps he only feared the one thing more than the other."

He gave her a look she recognized from the study in Rome, the look of a man who had heard the shape of a warning inside an observation and chosen, for the moment, to set it aside instead of answering it. He turned instead toward Philotas, already moving into the brisk cataloguing of business: which chambers would serve for the tribunal, which for records, whether the previous prefect's correspondence had been left in any order a sane man could use. Claudia let herself fall behind, trailing her fingers along the cool tesserae of a half-scraped mosaic bird, its wing still intact, its head already gone to bare plaster.

She found her own rooms by following the sound of water, an old habit now, three weeks at sea having made her ear for it acute, and came upon a small private courtyard at the palace's eastern face, open to the sea, with a shallow basin at its center where someone had let rainwater collect, undisturbed, greenish and still. She stood at its edge for a long moment, watching nothing move on its surface, before a voice behind her broke the quiet.

"The basin needs draining, Domina. It draws mosquitoes this time of year." The words were correct, careful, the accented Latin of someone who had learned it as a second language and later than childhood. "I can have it done before evening, if you'd rather it stay dry."

## The Woman at the Basin

She was perhaps Claudia's own age, dressed without ornament, dark hair bound back beneath a cloth in a style Claudia had not yet learned to read. Married or unmarried, devout or merely modest, she could not yet tell which. She stood at the courtyard's edge with the particular stillness of someone who had already decided, before Claudia turned, exactly how much of herself she intended to show a Roman stranger.

"You're of the household staff," Claudia said.

"Tamar." She did not offer more, no patronymic, no claim on a family name that might mean something to a Roman ear. "I kept this courtyard for the wife of the last prefect's steward, when there was a wife to keep it for. Gratus's men mostly left the water running. They said it was restful."

"And you don't."

"I say it draws mosquitoes." A small, precise smile, there and gone. "Restful is a Roman word for standing water, Domina. We have other words for it."

Claudia felt something in her, tired from three weeks of ocean and a day of strange rooms, sharpen with interest despite itself, the same alertness she had once felt as a child measuring a summons for danger before she measured it for anything else, though this time the alertness carried no fear in it at all, only a curiosity she had not expected a stranger's plain correction to provoke in her on the first day of an entirely new life. "What word would you use?"

Tamar considered the question with a seriousness Claudia would come to learn was characteristic of her, a refusal to treat even a stranger's idle question as beneath a real answer. "Stagnant. Or worse. Water that isn't moving toward anything isn't resting, in my father's telling. It's only forgotten." She glanced toward the basin. "Living water runs. That's the word we actually use for it: mayim chayim. Water still connected to its source. Anything else is a jar somebody meant to empty and didn't."

"The fountain in my husband's garden in Rome," Claudia said slowly, half to herself, "ran from a bronze dolphin's mouth into a stone basin, all day, every day, and I called that peace."

"Perhaps it was peace. Rome can afford to let water sit and call it beautiful." Something in Tamar's face came close to mockery without quite arriving there, and Claudia understood, for the first time since landing, that she had met someone in this province who did not intend to be impressed by her. "Here, water that doesn't move is water somebody has to explain."

Claudia found she did not mind the small correction, delivered without deference and without apology. She had spent five years of marriage learning to read the careful blankness of household slaves and the practiced humility of Roman clients angling for favor. This felt, oddly, like being spoken to by an equal for the first time since Ostia.

"Drain it, then," Claudia said. "Since it isn't resting anything."

"I will." Tamar's eyes moved briefly to the doorway, where the sound of Pontius's voice carried faintly from the audience hall, brisk with the business of inventory. "Your husband will want the household running by his own clock. Meals at Roman hours, the kitchen fires lit before dawn." A pause, weighed carefully. "I should tell you now, Domina, rather than let him discover it from someone less willing to say it outright. There are days I will not carry water for you, and food I will not prepare. It is not laziness, and it is not defiance of Rome. It is the Sabbath, and it will fall on your household whether your husband has accounted for it in his ledgers or not."

Claudia studied her for a moment: the flat, unapologetic honesty of it, offered before it could be discovered and resented instead of after. It reminded her, faintly and without warning, of Pontius on the evening he had first come to her in the receiving hall in Rome, admitting he had nothing clever to say and offering the truth in its place. She had liked him for it then. She found she liked this now, for much the same reason.

"How many days a year," Claudia asked, "will Rome have to account for that it hasn't accounted for yet?"

"More than your husband has drawn lines for, I expect." Tamar's mouth curved in something that fell short of a smile. "Your cartographers are very good at roads, Domina. I've heard the garrison clerks say so themselves. But there are things in this land that were old before Rome had a name for itself, and no map I've ever seen troubles to mark them."

Claudia thought of the parchment unrolled across the study table in Rome, the fine black script of tax yields, the wooden eagle planted over the dense ink cluster of Jerusalem, the wide blank margins where her own question had gone unanswered. *Where are the sacred boundaries. The lines your cartographer left out.*

"I don't imagine it has," she said quietly.

"There will be other things," Tamar added, rising and wiping her hands on the cloth at her belt. "Meat your kitchen buys from the wrong butcher. Milk set too near meat in the same dish. Small matters, Domina, to a Roman table. I don't ask you to keep them. I ask only that when I decline to serve something, you understand it isn't insolence, and don't let your husband's stewards mistake it for insolence either, or I'll be dismissed within the month for a fault that was never mine."

"I'll remember it," Claudia said, and meant it as more than courtesy. She thought of her guardian's tablinum in Rome, of a child's ink-stained hands and the lesson folded inside that room: that a place in someone else's house was earned by being useful. She understood, with an unexpected twist of recognition, that Tamar had likely learned some version of the same lesson long before either of them had met. "I should be just as direct with you in return. My husband keeps house by ledger and hour. He will not always remember to ask what a thing costs someone before he orders it done."

"No," Tamar agreed. "Men who draw straight lines rarely do." She said it without heat, the way one might note a fact about weather, and Claudia found she could not entirely disagree.

Somewhere beyond the courtyard wall, the sea went on breaking against Herod's stone harbor, patient and unhurried, the way it had broken against this coast for longer than either empire had existed to notice it. Claudia thought, listening to it, that she had crossed a great deal of water these past three weeks to arrive at a courtyard where a stranger's plain honesty felt more like home already than anything the palace's borrowed marble had offered her since she stepped off the gangway. Tamar knelt at the basin's edge and began, without being asked twice, to open the small channel that would let the standing water run down toward the sea instead of settling where it was. Claudia watched the water go, and this time did not look for her own face in it before it left.
