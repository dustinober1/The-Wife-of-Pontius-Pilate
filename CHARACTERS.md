# Characters

Cast sheet for continuity. Facts marked **(on page)** are already locked by drafted prose (Prologue, Ch1, Ch2) and cannot be contradicted; everything else is a working decision that can still be adjusted — but once a chapter drafts it, move it to **(on page)**.

## Principals

### Claudia

- **Role:** Protagonist; default POV. Wife of Pontius Pilate.
- **Born:** ~A.D. 1–2 (derived: age 9 in the tablinum scene, betrothal talk 8 years later, married ~A.D. 19 — Ch1 states "seven years of marriage" in A.D. 26).
- **Ages at key points:** ~24–25 at the appointment (Ch1) · ~31–32 at the trial (Prologue/Ch23) · ~35 at the recall (Ch29) · ~36 in the Epilogue.
- **Background (on page):** Ward of an unnamed noble house in Rome; her blood "claimed, tolerated, but never quite plain." Staff called her "the ward of the house." Raised to be useful rather than beloved — the ink-stained-hands lesson (Ch2). An uncle once passed through Jerusalem serving under the governor of Syria (her source for Judaea knowledge in Ch1). Nursed as a child by Sulpicia.
- **Appearance:** Deliberately undescribed. The book renders Claudia only through action, sensation, and interior perception, never physical description — keep it that way through Ch29; do not introduce a mirror/appearance scene as a shortcut to characterization.
- **Habits/tells (on page):** Presses her thumb against the seam of her palm; checks the old ink-stain crescent under her thumbnail even when her hands are clean. Measures a summons for danger before anything else (childhood instinct, named in Ch2). Reads men's motives behind their titles — Pontius explicitly recruits this skill (Ch1).
- **Voice:** Even, precise, watchful. Argues by image (the fountain, the unmapped pride, the cliff beside the road) rather than by escalation. Interior register runs to water metaphors.
- **Arc:** Usefulness → conscience → grief → an earned turn toward belief that reads equally as faith or moral awakening (see CLAUDE.md faith arc — never doctrinal, never overtly supernatural).

### Pontius Pilate

- **Role:** Prefect of Judaea. POV chapters 4, 6, 9, 15, 16, 19, 25, 28 (per OUTLINE.md table).
- **Born:** ~10 B.C. (working; must satisfy: gray already threading his temples at the first meeting ~A.D. 18 "despite looking younger," and his Ch1 ambition of a praetorship "before I am forty" reached by ~A.D. 29). Ages: ~mid-30s at appointment, ~early 40s at the trial, ~mid-40s at recall.
- **Rank/politics (on page):** Equestrian, not senatorial — the old families' condescension drives him. Rose "on borrowed favor" by attaching himself to powerful men in the Praetorian Guard; Sejanus personally secured the Judaea commission. Succeeds Valerius Gratus (11 years in post).
- **Appearance (on page):** Broad; dark hair with gray threading the temples since his late 20s; dark eyes; calloused thumb; bronze clasp at his collar at the trial; crimson wool mantle and iron-studded caligae for travel.
- **Theory of rule (on page):** "An even hand and an unbending spine." "Rome governs by discovering how [provinces] are alike." Trusts everything that can be counted — roads, revenues, ranks, force. Plans from maps and ledgers; frames politics as engineering. The book erodes this theory one crisis at a time.
- **Tells (on page):** Tension in the neck, fingers twitching near his belt when trapped; sets his goblet down with a deliberate click when challenged; public formality vs. rare private tenderness (thumb along Claudia's cheekbone). His fear is not of Judaea but of the men in Rome who staked their names on him.
- **First-meeting note (on page):** Bowed a half-beat too quickly, voice cracked, admitted he'd thought of nothing clever, then: "I am afraid of Rome forgetting me." Honesty under nerves is the buried good in him — worth one late echo when everything else is stripped away.

### Marcus

- **Role:** Household secretary/steward. The witness-figure: present at nearly every major public moment; his small reactions are how the reader sees Claudia's conscience forming (see CLAUDE.md threads).
- **Status (on page):** Greek; a slave (the Prologue notes "a slave interrupting a capital tribunal risked the lash"). Called "boy" in the Prologue — keep in mind this is also period address for a male slave regardless of age, which gives flexibility.
- **Born:** ~A.D. 10 (working) — mid-teens at the Ostia crossing where he's already managing the baggage inventory against a wax tablet, ~23 at the trial. "Slender," quick, careful.
- **On page:** Ink-stain on his thumb (mirror of Claudia's motif — deliberate; use sparingly). Jaw tightens rather than speaks. At the trial he delivers the note through the legal scribe at the foot of the dais, not to a guard.
- **Open decisions:** His literacy history (did Claudia teach him? natural fit with the ink motif but not yet on page); his fate in the Epilogue.
- **Ch27 update:** Near thirty by A.D. 36. Has begun keeping his own small store of philosophy and history scrolls, read privately by lamplight, bought with his own wages — his own version of Claudia's private practice of keeping something the household's duties don't ask of him. Tells Claudia he no longer trusts himself to be only what his duties say he is. Still unresolved: what he does with this, and his fate in the Epilogue.

### Tamar

- **Role:** Jewish household confidante in Caesarea; Claudia's window into Jewish law and hope "on human terms, not political ones" (Ch7, on page). Later the household's channel for news of John and of Jesus (Ch8, 18, 20).
- **Name:** Confirmed as Tamar (no longer provisional).
- **Position:** Free hired servant, not enslaved — chosen deliberately so the friendship with Claudia can read as between near-equals rather than mistress/property, and because it's historically plausible in a coastal household with a mixed local staff.
- **Family (on page, Ch7):** Local to Caesarea, not Galilee or Jerusalem. Mother died of a fever when Tamar was small; raised afterward by her aunt Rivkah in a house two streets from the harbor. Rivkah dies of a lung ailment in Ch7, giving Claudia her first close look at Jewish mourning customs (the seven days, torn collar, round mourners' bread, no thanks offered for it). A cousin, Yaakov, stood in the standards protest (Ch5, gray shawl, three rows back from the gate) and appears again bruised but unharmed in Ch7. Injured in the aqueduct riot (Ch10): a club across the shoulder, ribs uncertain under the physician's care; survives, keeps the arm.
- **Relation to the Jesus movement:** None directly — she relays what ordinary people hear and hope, secondhand. Keeps the faith arc unforced and consistent with CLAUDE.md's rule against a doctrinal conversion scene. Ch7 gives her the clearest statement yet of the book's "human terms" version of hope: not fear of Roman punishment, but a belief that "what is broken is not broken forever," offered to Claudia as something to carry back to Pontius without letting him mistake it for strategy.

## Household

- **Sulpicia (on page):** Claudia's old nurse; refused to let her cross the sea unattended. Elderly in A.D. 26 — decide whether she survives to Part IV or dies in Judaea (a quiet early grief could seed Claudia's larger ones).
- **Two enslaved women (on page):** From the Roman household, unnamed at Ostia. Name them if they recur.
- **Ennius (on page):** The guardian's steward in Rome, Ch2 childhood scenes only. Never raised his voice above a murmur.
- **The guardian (on page):** Unnamed. Flat voice of a man "accustomed to being obeyed rather than loved"; cold hands; "Do you know what it means to be useful to this house?" Keep him unnamed — he works as a force, not a person. If Ch27/Epilogue needs word of his death, that's available as a beat.
- **Pontius's legal scribe (on page):** At the foot of the dais at the trial; the link in the note's chain of custody. Unnamed; could be given a face in the Pontius POV chapters.
- **Simon (on page, Ch8):** The cook's boy; quiet, previously unremarked in the household. Walks to the Jordan with his uncle and is baptized by John; gives Claudia her only near-firsthand account of the preacher (John offstage per the style rule, reported through Simon rather than shown directly). Open thread: whether he recurs.

## Historical figures (as the book needs them)

- **Tiberius:** Emperor throughout; withdrawn to Capri from ~A.D. 26–27 — all imperial contact is by letter, which the book should exploit (distance, delay, dread). Dies 16 March A.D. 37, before Pontius can answer for Gerizim.
- **Sejanus:** Praetorian prefect; Pontius's patron and the marriage's political ceiling. Falls 18 October A.D. 31 (Ch16). His letters in Ch11/15 should read, in retrospect, like a man overreaching. **First letter on page in Ch11**: reaches Caesarea three weeks after the aqueduct riot; calls the suppression "competence," absolves Pontius of the deaths as the cost of compelling order, and instructs him to continue the works and keep the treasury accounts beyond a priest's later complaint. Read by Claudia as arithmetic dressed in a patron's confident hand — no acknowledgment of the dead by name or number. **Ch15 (Pontius POV, not yet fallen)**: Senate dispatch announces Sejanus's joint consulship with Tiberius for the coming year, statues in the theater of Pompey, his birthday added to the public calendar, and his name added to the provincial new year's oath alongside the Emperor's — the oath Pontius administers at Caesarea's forum. Pontius reads it as confirmation of a four-year bet on borrowed favor and writes north angling for eventual recall to duties "closer to the center of things." Philotas warns, unheeded, that binding the province's oaths to a name other than the Emperor's hands critics "a new thread to pull" if that name ever comes loose — direct setup for Ch16-17. **Falls on page in Ch16**: news reaches Caesarea five weeks late (18 October execution; dispatch from Macro's office arrives via an out-of-season ship). Pontius's Ch15 letter angling for advancement is already unrecallable, sent into the same weeks as the unmasking. He orders the oath and coinage timing scrubbed from the record, holds a corrective public sacrifice for Tiberius alone in the same forum, and learns from Rufus that the purge's reach is still unknown — no news naming him either way. Sejanus is not named as a character again after this chapter except in retrospect.
- **Caiaphas:** High priest A.D. 18–36; survivor; his working arrangement with the prefecture is the unspoken machinery behind Ch4, 9, 17, 23. Deposed by Vitellius shortly after Pontius's recall — a quiet irony available to the Epilogue. **First on-page appearance in Ch9**: receives Pontius in a chamber off the Temple's outer court (a deliberately neutral location neither man names as a concession); authorizes korbanas funds for the aqueduct on the condition his own overseers watch the spending alongside Pontius's engineers. Reads to Pontius as a man who calculates rather than believes, which Pontius finds easier to negotiate with than conviction. Names Corban for him: an offering "set apart," no longer available to ordinary use, which is the vocabulary the priesthood will later use against the spending even though Caiaphas himself authorized it. **Ch17, post-Sejanus**: reverses the visiting custom by coming to Pontius's own hall in Caesarea with a minimal retinue, announces the aqueduct's clean completion, and states plainly that "good faith has become a scarcer currency" this season — a recalibration of leverage delivered without any direct threat, understood by both men and by Claudia (watching from the gallery) as the unspoken groundwork for what Rome would hear about Pontius if the priesthood ever chose to write.
- **Eleazar:** Younger Temple-appointed priest, the priesthood's senior representative on the Ch17 joint Roman/Temple cistern-maintenance commission (Pontius's own concession, a first). Confident, procedurally sharp, invokes Pontius's own authority against a Roman overseer mid-session as a small demonstration of the new balance of power — a first on-page sign of the recalibration reaching below Caiaphas to junior priesthood figures. **Recurs in Ch19**: requests a private word after a commission sitting to warn Pontius, without asking him to act, that the Sanhedrin has taken note of the Galilean teacher and worries about the size of the following he may bring to Passover; frames it as a courtesy from "one office to another" while making plain the council would rather Rome's instrument handle a delicate matter than dirty its own hands. Consistent with his Ch17 procedural confidence, tempered here by genuine unease. Open thread: whether he recurs again through the trial chapters.
- **Annas:** Former high priest, Caiaphas's father-in-law; real power behind the house.
- **Herod Antipas:** Tetrarch of Galilee and Perea. Executes John at Machaerus. Rivalry with Pontius (Luke 23:12 — "became friends that day") available for Ch23 if wanted; Ch12 plants the rivalry as wary, performed cordiality, not yet friendship. **First appearance in person in Ch12**, at Tiberias: receives Pontius and Claudia to settle a border grazing dispute; reads to Claudia as a man who survives Roman counterparts by conceding small points and concealing what he actually wants until he already has it. Holds John confined (not yet executed) at Machaerus; visits the prisoner at night and comes away, per Salome, looking questioned rather than the questioner.
- **Herodias:** Formerly married to Antipas's half-brother; the scandal John denounced. **On page in Ch12**, at her court in Tiberias: composed, watchful, reads as reconnaissance disguised as hospitality. Names John to Claudia as the one danger worth naming aloud; pushes Antipas to either execute or release him, unable to bear a prisoner "unsilenced." Her fear of losing the argument to a man who "wants nothing" and so can't be bribed or frightened is the clearest statement yet of what will end John's life in Ch13.
- **Salome:** Herodias's daughter (name from Josephus, not the Gospels). **On page in Ch12**, ~16–17, composure not yet fully mastered like her mother's. Speaks to Claudia in the colonnade at Tiberias more candidly than anyone else in the household; relays secondhand household gossip about John's manner in confinement (silent, waiting, not raging or begging) and reads her mother's naming-him-aloud habit as Herodias's only weapon against an opponent she can't otherwise defeat.
- **John the Baptist:** Never on page directly (rumor in Ch8, confined at Machaerus per Ch12 secondhand report, news of death in Ch13) — keep him offstage; his offstage-ness is the model for how the book handles Jesus too. **Death reported in Ch13**, roughly a year after Ch12: news reaches Caesarea already shaped into the birthday-feast/dancing-daughter/oath version (Gospel-flavored rumor, reported secondhand and explicitly held uncertain — Tamar notes both the oath story and a preemption motive circulate in the market, "and neither reason makes the man any less dead than the other would"). Simon (Ch8) grieves him directly, having been baptized by him; Claudia declines to simplify Salome into villain or victim when Simon asks about her.
- **Jesus of Nazareth, Pontius's provisional assessment (Ch19):** Reports reach Caesarea of a crowd of five thousand fed near Bethsaida and dispersed peaceably at his word; Pontius reads the miracle claim skeptically but treats the crowd's discipline on dispersal as the operative fact. His movement stays confined to Galilee (Herod's jurisdiction), speaks of "a kingdom" in terms his informants can't translate into a concrete political threat, and has said nothing against tribute, the census, or Roman authority. Pontius writes Herod a measured dispatch declining to treat the tetrarch's guilty conscience over John as a reliable threat assessment, while privately noting the key difference from John: John was fixed to one place and could be measured like a road; this teacher moves, which makes his numbers unverifiable and the risk harder to bound. Judgment as of Ch19: not yet a threat, but worth watching into Passover, especially given Eleazar's warning the same chapter that the Sanhedrin already watches him closely.
- **Jesus of Nazareth (on page, Prologue):** Bound with coarse rope, simple tunic torn at the collar, unnaturally still, "a quiet authority that made the imperial iron around him look fragile." Seen only at distance and through others — preserve this remove everywhere except Ch23's extension past the Prologue.
- **Joseph of Arimathea:** Receives the body (Ch25). On page: a Sanhedrin member who did not consent to the previous night's vote; petitions Pontius alone, without retinue, the evening before the Sabbath, for the body and permission to bury it in his own tomb rather than let it go to the common ground. Pontius grants it after weighing (and finding no) ulterior calculation in the request; Joseph's stated reason is that he'd rather carry the cost of his belief openly than leave it unspoken, an answer Pontius privately can't stop turning over afterward. Confirmed dead by a centurion (a spear to the side, quicker than the other two) before Pontius signs the release.
- **Ch25 (The Empty Report), locked for Ch26 cross-check:** The morning after the crucifixion (the Sabbath), Caiaphas comes to Pontius personally to request a Roman guard and the council's seal on the tomb, citing the dead man's own reported claim that he would rise on the third day, and fear his followers might steal the body to fake it. Pontius finds Caiaphas's evident unease disproportionate to a claim the high priest says he doesn't credit, and grants the guard for the same low-cost-to-himself reasons he grants most requests this season. Pontius's official dispatch to Rome is deliberately flattened bureaucratic language ("no substantive threat to Roman order... sentence was carried out according to law... the province remains at peace") that omits everything that actually happened; he notices and is unsettled by how easily the flattening came to him. Sets up Ch26: the guard is already posted and sealed before any tomb rumor reaches the household.
- **Vitellius:** Legate of Syria; removes Pontius after Gerizim (Ch29); sends Marcellus as caretaker. **First on-page action in Ch28**: the Samaritan council's delegation reaches him in Antioch within a month (unusually fast for a Judaean grievance); his dispatch to Pontius is unadorned and damning, naming the mountain gathering an unarmed pilgrimage answered by cavalry before any crime had been committed. Orders Pontius to Rome to answer to Tiberius directly and sends Marcellus ahead as caretaker.

- **Ch21 (Into Jerusalem), locked for Ch22–23 cross-check:** The household travels up from Caesarea to "the upper palace," Herod the Great's old residence and the governor's traditional feast-week seat in Jerusalem (distinct from the Caesarea praetorium). Pontius doubles the escorting cohort and has Rufus reinforce the Antonia garrison's watch on the Temple's outer colonnades before the feast even begins, on account of the unusually large pilgrim crowds. The triumphal entry (colt, branches, "son of David" acclamations) is witnessed by Claudia at a distance from the palace terrace and confirmed by Marcus and Rufus's reports: no weapons, no word against Rome, the crowd dispersing peaceably into the Temple courts. Tamar reports a new watchfulness among the priesthood, the council meeting later into the night than usual. The arrest itself happens offstage: past the middle watch four nights into the stay, Marcus wakes Claudia to report Temple guards moved out past the Kidron gate in force and have not returned, with an unconfirmed kitchen rumor that they went to take the teacher into custody in the garden groves across the valley. Pontius is not yet told (Marcus judges it not yet his office's matter). Chapter ends before dawn, with Claudia unable to sleep, unconsciously connecting the report to her Ch20 dream image without saying so.

- **Ch23 (The Bema), locked:** Rejoins and reuses the Prologue's "View from the Balcony," "The Summons and the Message," and "The Knife-Edge Moment" sections nearly verbatim, then extends past them. Pontius questions Jesus privately (kingship neither fully claimed nor denied, described as belonging to "a different order of things"); sends him briefly to Herod Antipas (in Jerusalem for the feast), who sends him back within the hour no wiser, the two men parting "on friendlier terms than they have managed in years" (fulfills the Luke 23:12 "became friends that day" beat flagged as available in CHARACTERS.md). Barabbas is named as the insurrectionist/killer the crowd chooses for release. **Water thread, third appearance**: Pontius washes his hands in a basin before the court, declares himself innocent of the man's blood, the council answers that they accept the charge on themselves and their children (paraphrased, not quoted); Claudia later stares at the basin's water and consciously connects it back to the Ostia black water and the garden fountain (thread's third of four planned appearances — one more at the Epilogue only). He orders the flogging and the crucifixion sentence; neither is depicted, only reported and reacted to. Closing scene: Pontius comes to Claudia on the terrace an hour later, admits he gave the order with her note still folded against his chest, and that he doesn't expect to set the map down again in the old confident way (echoes the Ch16 map vow — this is dialogue only, not a physical map scene, so it doesn't violate the "no map again before the Epilogue" rule). Sets up Ch24 to cover the crucifixion entirely offstage/at remove.

- **Ch24 (What the City Heard), locked:** The crucifixion happens entirely offstage; Claudia never leaves the palace. The sky darkens unnaturally past the sixth hour (noted by Tamar, paraphrased Gospel detail, held ambiguous as sign vs. season). Marcus goes to the execution hill on his own initiative and reports back: three men crucified, a crowd larger but quieter than expected, a woman identified to him as Jesus's mother present with a few others (most followers scattered "sometime in the night"), some final words too distant to hear clearly and reported secondhand, no disturbance among the crowd. Simon and Tamar each process the day's grief in dialogue with Claudia, both landing on hope/mending as something that must be carried rather than resolved. That evening Pontius tells Claudia he has already granted a council member's (unnamed on page, i.e. Joseph of Arimathea per the outline, not yet named directly) petition to take the body down before the Sabbath and bury it in his own tomb rather than the common ground. Chapter ends with both of them keeping watch again from the terrace at nightfall, the sky returned to normal, the pavement swept clean. Sets up Ch25 (Pontius POV) to cover the official paperwork aftermath.

- **Ch26 (Rumors of an Empty Tomb), locked:** Third day after the crucifixion. Rufus reports the tomb guard's confused story (a struck-senseless account vs. a sleeping account) and that Caiaphas's people are already paying the soldiers to swear to the sleeping/body-theft version. Pontius dismisses the whole matter as an obvious theft dressed in a convenient story, closes it without a second thought, and privately files it the same way he's filed every crisis since Sejanus fell. Tamar relays the women-at-the-tomb account secondhand (via her cousin's wife and the sister-in-law who was reportedly present), including the detail that the two nearest witnesses disagree on one vs. two figures at the tomb but agree on everything else — deliberately held as uncertain, secondhand, human-scale testimony, never confirmed on page. Marcus adds that the garrison mess is laughing at the guards' cover story without believing it. **Major turning point**: that evening Claudia writes a private page and, for the first time since she began this practice (the burned letters of Ch11, Ch14, Ch20), does not burn it — she rolls it and locks it in her keepsake chest alongside the brass garden key from Ch1 and a ribbon from her own wedding. This is the first physically preserved record of her private doubt/faith and marks the turn from "unable to name" to "unwilling to let it go unrecorded." Any later chapter revisiting her keepsake chest (Epilogue especially) must treat this page as still there.

- **Ch27 (Three Years of Silence), locked:** Time skip from Passover A.D. 33 to early A.D. 36, Pontius's tenth year in office. He governs harder and faster, closing disputes without full hearings, reads every dispatch with the same flattened attention regardless of weight; still refuses to discuss the trial directly, admitting only that he "carries the accounting of it," and privately envies Claudia whatever lets her sleep, not knowing hers doesn't come easily either. Claudia now keeps four unburned private pages in her keepsake chest (adds to the one from Ch26) and has begun quietly using her position to help individuals harmed by Pontius's harder rule (a conscripted widow's son reassigned, a family's unjust tax assessment appealed through Philotas) — small, deliberately unheroic gestures, framed as the point rather than a failure of scale. First mention of the brewing Samaritan/Gerizim matter: Pontius dismisses it as a "fool's errand" (a crowd digging for Moses' buried vessels, not crowning a king) and reads its danger by the same crowd-measuring logic he once applied to the Galilean in Ch19 — Claudia notes the parallel and doesn't share his confidence. Sets up Ch28.

- **Ch28 (Gerizim), locked:** Early A.D. 36. A Samaritan prophet gathers several thousand at Tirathana, meaning to climb Gerizim to uncover vessels supposedly buried by Moses. Pontius, contrary to his careful Ch19 assessment of the Galilean crowd, acts fast without verifying details (no sketch requested from Philotas this time, no check on whether the crowd is armed for war or just carrying ordinary road weapons) — the deliberate ironic inversion of his earlier restraint, product of three hardened years since Ch27. He recalls Claudia's skepticism (from Ch27's "fool's errand" conversation) mid-ride and dismisses it. Cavalry and infantry intercept before the climb is a third done; the result is a rout of unarmed pilgrims, many killed fleeing or trampled rather than in real combat; captured leaders executed without hearing under Pontius's standing order. Rufus (serving him roughly ten years by this point) delivers the report and is the first to voice doubt that this will read as justified. The Samaritan council's complaint reaches Vitellius in Antioch unusually fast; Vitellius's dispatch orders Pontius to Rome to answer to Tiberius directly, with Marcellus sent ahead as caretaker. Chapter ends with Pontius's private recognition, too late to help anyone, that he traded one failure (not mapping the province's pride, Ch1's phrase) for another (never mapping his own fear) — deliberately light-touch reference to the compass/map thread via dialogue-memory only, no physical map scene, keeping the "no map again before the Epilogue" rule intact.

## Locked continuity facts (from drafted prose — cross-check before touching Ch21–23)

- The note's exact wording: *"Have nothing to do with that innocent man, for I have suffered much today because of a dream."* Dictated to Marcus, written in Greek, rolled tight before the ink dried.
- Chain of custody: Marcus → Pontius's legal scribe at the foot of the dais → Pontius, mid-sentence.
- Geography: Claudia's terrace is ~forty yards from the tribunal, above the paved courtyard "the Greeks called Lithostrotos." The locked gaze across that distance ends the Prologue.
- Claudia at the trial: barefoot, silk palla over linen tunic, hairpins and gold left behind.
- The brass key to the Roman garden gate came with her to Ostia (Ch1) — available as a late object callback if wanted.
- Pontius's marker on the map: a wooden imperial eagle placed on Jerusalem; the iron compass Claudia set into his hand (Ch1). **Second map scene used in Ch16**: the night the Sejanus news lands, Pontius sets the compass down beside the map rather than on it and tells Claudia he no longer trusts what the map shows him — the theory that every problem reduces to measurement has just failed on the one variable (a patron's favor) he never thought to measure. He says he won't unroll the map again "not soon." Per CLAUDE.md's thread plan, no map appears again before the Epilogue, where its absence is the point.
- **The dream, first seeding (Ch14):** a sharp cracking sound like stone splitting under ice; a height with no fixed shape (terrace, hillside, or neither); Pontius moving toward an edge no law can protect him from crossing; a faceless, unnamed presence standing at the edge, its stillness a waiting rather than a threat, turning toward Claudia just before the sound wakes her. Recurs once, thinner, eleven nights later, then goes quiet for the rest of the year. Claudia tells no one but Tamar (obliquely) and a private letter she burns unread by anyone else. Deliberately unidentified with "the Galilean" — Jesus is not named in the household until Ch18, so the figure in this dream must stay faceless and unlabeled. Ch20 ("The Second Dream") is where it sharpens next.
- **The dream, second seeding (Ch20):** Set roughly a year after Ch18–19 (~A.D. 32), two weeks before departure for Passover A.D. 33, nearly two years after the first dream (Ch14). Same cracking sound, same unfixed height, same edge, but the presence now has a definite outline for the first time: bound at the wrists, upright, "patient" rather than defiant, still turning toward Claudia just before the sound wakes her. Still no face. Claudia burns a second private page (same practice as Ch14/Ch11) that admits, for the first time in writing, that the fear "has begun, on its own account, to take a shape," before burning it unfinished. In the kitchen-court scene with Tamar, news arrives that the teacher is traveling south for Passover with a growing crowd already calling him "son of David" in the open road (the Jericho blind man episode, paraphrased). Claudia privately connects the dream's shape to the teacher for the first time but does not speak the thought aloud and does not tell Pontius about the dream. Marcus's thread-beat: brings watered wine unasked a second morning, his quiet noticing again unremarked upon directly. Ends with Pontius naming the "son of David" rumor as a live governing concern for the Jerusalem trip, setting up Ch21. Per the locked dream note, the figure must stay faceless through this chapter; full identification as "the Galilean" is reserved for Ch22/the Prologue.
- **Jesus named in the household (Ch18):** set the second winter/spring after Sejanus's fall (~A.D. 32). Herod Antipas's dispatch to Pontius is the first mention, fearing the teacher might be John "raised back into the world"; Pontius resists building policy on a tetrarch's guilty nerves but privately isn't fully convinced by his own distinction. Tamar relays secondhand reports (via her cousin's wife's kin near Capernaum): synagogue teaching "as though the words applied to the room," healings of complaints families had stopped expecting to mend, and table fellowship with tax farmers offered before repentance rather than after, the explicit contrast with John's model. Yaakov (Tamar's cousin, previously in the standards protest and injured in the aqueduct riot) reads this as exhaustion with a wages-for-repentance theology rather than settled belief. Simon reports his uncle's news that some of John's own followers have gone to this teacher, and relays an unconfirmed report that Jesus was himself among those John baptized before either had a public name, the first on-page link between the two men beyond rumor. Chapter ends with Claudia privately repeating the name to herself, unable to say why it lodges differently than any earlier rumor; Marcus's hesitation at the doorway is his thread-beat for this chapter.
